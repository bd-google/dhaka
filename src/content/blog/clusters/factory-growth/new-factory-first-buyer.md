### The chicken-and-egg problem every new factory faces

Serious buyers screen for track record and existing client references (see our piece on <a href="/blog/factory-buyer-access">how buyers actually screen factories</a>) — which is a real problem for a genuinely capable new factory with no export history to point to yet. This isn't a reason a new factory can't succeed; it's a reason the first buyer relationship needs a different approach than the tenth one does.

### What actually substitutes for track record early on

Verifiable registration and compliance documentation carries proportionally more weight when there's no order history to back it up — a new factory with clean, complete, currently-valid documentation (trade license, BIN, RJSC, and any relevant compliance certification already in place before the first order, not scrambled together after) signals seriousness even without a client list.

Starting with a smaller, lower-risk order from a buyer willing to take a chance on a new factory — rather than immediately pursuing a large program — builds the first genuine reference and track record faster than holding out for a large order no unproven factory is likely to win first.

### What actually works against a new factory unintentionally

Overpromising capacity or timeline to win that crucial first order (see our piece on <a href="/blog/capacity-overpromising">capacity overpromising</a>) is a worse mistake for a new factory than for an established one — a new factory has no accumulated goodwill to draw on if the first order goes badly, and a damaged first impression is much harder to recover from without an existing reputation to fall back on.
