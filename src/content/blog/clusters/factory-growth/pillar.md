### The misconception, stated plainly

A lot of factories — and a lot of buyers — assume "the factory is certified" means "everything the factory produces is certified." It doesn't. This single misunderstanding is behind a real, recurring category of problems: a factory genuinely, validly certified for its own operations still can't legitimately deliver a certified product if something broke upstream, before the material ever reached the cutting table.

### The two documents that actually matter, and why they're not the same thing

Certification schemes like GOTS and GRS work through two separate documents:

**A Scope Certificate (SC)** is issued to a specific facility after an audit, confirming that facility is approved to handle certified material for specific processes and product categories. Think of it as proof the factory passed inspection and is licensed to work with certified goods — it says nothing about any particular batch of material actually in the building right now.

**A Transaction Certificate (TC)** is issued for a specific shipment or batch, confirming that specific material actually is certified, and tracking it as it moves from one certified operator to the next. This is the document that proves your current order's yarn is genuinely certified — not just that your factory is generally approved to work with certified materials.

### Where the chain actually breaks

If any operator upstream in this specific order's chain — most commonly the yarn spinner, but potentially a dye house or trader as well — isn't currently certified, or their certificate has lapsed, the chain breaks at that exact point. No amount of downstream processing at a fully certified cut-and-sew factory restores it. This means a factory can hold a completely legitimate, currently valid GOTS Scope Certificate and still be unable to honestly claim a specific order as GOTS-certified, if that order's yarn came from an uncertified or lapsed-certification spinner.

### Why this causes real, documented problems

Buyers who don't understand this distinction sometimes accept a factory's own Scope Certificate as sufficient proof for an entire order, without asking for the batch-specific Transaction Certificate. When the gap surfaces later — during a buyer's own audit, or a retail compliance review — it looks like fraud on the factory's part, even in cases where the factory's own certification was completely genuine and the actual break happened at a yarn supplier the factory doesn't fully control.

### What a factory should actually do

- **When a buyer requests certified product, don't default to presenting your own Scope Certificate as the answer.** Confirm the specific yarn or fabric batch for that order came from a currently-certified supplier.
- **Request the Transaction Certificate for that specific batch from your yarn/fabric supplier**, and pass it along to your buyer — this is the document that actually proves the claim, not your own facility certificate.
- **If your regular yarn supplier isn't certified**, source certified yarn specifically for orders that require it, even if it costs more or requires a different supplier relationship than your standard production. Trying to fulfill a certified order with uncertified input material is the single most common way this problem happens.
- **Keep your own Scope Certificate current and don't let it lapse mid-relationship** — a factory that lets certification expire and doesn't proactively flag it to affected buyers erodes trust even faster than a factory that's honest about a supply-chain gap upfront.

### Why getting this right is a genuine competitive advantage

A factory that proactively explains this distinction to buyers — rather than letting them assume a Scope Certificate covers everything — comes across as more sophisticated and trustworthy than one that either doesn't understand the distinction itself or hopes the buyer won't ask. In a market where compliance-focused buyers are increasingly asking for batch-specific proof (see our <a href="/blog/uflpa-cotton-traceability">UFLPA traceability piece</a> for a related, higher-stakes version of this same principle), being the factory that already has this documented and ready is a real differentiator.
