### Why this matters right now specifically

As covered in our piece on <a href="/blog/skilled-worker-turnover">skilled worker turnover during the current crisis</a>, experienced operators are the hardest people to replace without a quality cost — and the current period of instability (power disruption, order volume pressure) gives skilled workers more reason to consider leaving for a more stable employer, if one is available.

### What actually helps beyond wages

Predictable, consistent shift scheduling matters more during a period when power outages are already disrupting normal patterns — workers who can plan their lives around a reliable schedule are less likely to leave even without a wage increase a factory may not be able to offer right now. Clear, visible skill-development paths (training toward higher-skilled roles, defined promotion criteria) give experienced workers a reason to stay and grow rather than assume their only path forward is switching employers.

### What actually works against retention, even unintentionally

Inconsistent overtime demands during production crunches, unclear communication about the factory's own stability during a difficult period (workers pick up on uncertainty even when it isn't announced), and treating skilled-operator departure as routine attrition rather than a specific, addressable retention problem worth actively managing.
