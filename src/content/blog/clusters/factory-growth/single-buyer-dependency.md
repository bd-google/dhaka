### Why this feels like success while it's happening

A large, reliable buyer providing consistent, high volume looks like exactly the stability every factory wants — and for a while, it is. The risk isn't in the relationship itself; it's in what happens to the factory if that buyer reduces orders, shifts sourcing elsewhere, or simply renegotiates from a position of strength once the factory has become financially dependent on that one relationship (see our piece on <a href="/blog/discount-after-shipment">post-shipment discount pressure</a> for one specific way this leverage gets used).

### Why this connects to the wider financial fragility pattern

A factory generating most of its revenue from one buyer has effectively handed that buyer significant influence over its own financial stability — the buyer's own sourcing decisions, made for reasons entirely outside the factory's control, can trigger exactly the order-shortage spiral covered in our <a href="/blog/order-shortage-spiral">order shortage piece</a>.

### What actually reduces this exposure without damaging the good relationship

Actively maintaining smaller, secondary buyer relationships even while a primary relationship is strong, rather than only building out a diversified buyer base after a large buyer has already pulled back. Treating buyer diversification as an ongoing part of business development, not a crisis response.
