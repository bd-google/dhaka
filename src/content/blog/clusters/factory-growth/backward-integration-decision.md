### Why this decision isn't automatically the right move for every factory

Vertical integration — a garment factory investing in its own yarn spinning or fabric dyeing — reduces dependence on external suppliers and can improve margin and lead-time control. It's also a genuinely large capital commitment, and given the debt-heavy financial structure already common across the sector (see our <a href="/blog/bank-debt-fragility">bank debt fragility piece</a>), taking on more leveraged investment isn't automatically the right move just because larger, established groups have done it successfully.

### When it tends to make sense

For a factory with consistent, high-volume demand in a specific fabric category — where buying the same fabric externally at scale already represents a major recurring cost — bringing that specific stage in-house can genuinely improve both margin and control. It tends to make the most sense when a factory has demonstrated stable, sustained demand in a narrow enough category that the investment has a clear, specific payback case, not as a general growth strategy.

### When it tends to be a mistake

Taking on this investment during a period of order volume uncertainty, or without a specific, demonstrated demand pattern justifying the specific capacity being added, adds fixed cost and debt exposure precisely when a factory can least afford reduced flexibility. The current environment — where working capital is already under real pressure (see our <a href="/blog/working-capital-crisis">working capital crisis piece</a>) — is a reason for particular caution before committing to a large backward-integration investment, not a reason to accelerate it to "catch up" with larger competitors.
