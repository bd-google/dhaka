### The number that matters most: 98%

Bangladesh's entire garment export industry — $39 billion a year, the second-largest in the world — runs on cotton the country barely grows itself. Bangladesh is roughly 98% import-dependent for raw cotton, and is the world's second-largest cotton importer after China. This single fact underlies almost everything downstream: yarn costs, fabric costs, and ultimately Bangladesh's competitiveness against India (which is cotton self-sufficient) and China (which dominates man-made fiber production).

### Where the gap actually is, by category

**Cotton fiber** — close to entirely imported. Domestic cotton cultivation exists but meets only a small fraction of demand; Bangladesh consumes something in the range of 8 to 8.5 million bales of raw cotton a year, against textile industry capacity to process closer to double that if supply were available.

**Yarn** — a large, genuine gap, though a real domestic spinning industry does exist. Recent trade data shows Bangladesh spending well over $2 billion annually on yarn imports, with India as the dominant supplier — not primarily because of price, but because of shorter shipment times and lower logistics costs as a neighboring country.

**Woven fabric** — the single largest structural gap in the entire value chain. Domestic mills cover the large majority of knit fabric demand, but woven fabric remains heavily import-dependent — estimates vary by year and source, generally placing imported woven fabric somewhere between 60% and 90% of total demand. China is the dominant supplier here, holding over 60% of Bangladesh's fabric import value in recent data.

**Man-made and technical fiber** — described in industry analysis as "critically weak" domestically. Even Bangladesh's genuine successes in technical product categories — the seamless knitwear machines behind the country's growing activewear and legging production, for instance — still rely on imported synthetic yarn, because the domestic synthetic fiber base isn't there yet.

**Dyes and chemicals** — sourced almost entirely from abroad, a fact that shows up directly in cost and lead-time exposure whenever global shipping or currency conditions shift.

### Why this matters more now than it did five years ago

Bangladesh's garment industry is under real pressure to move beyond basic, low-cost production — into technical textiles, more sophisticated wovens, and higher-value categories — partly because rising wages and competition from Vietnam and Cambodia are eroding its pure cost advantage. That shift requires exactly the inputs the country doesn't produce enough of itself: higher-spec yarns, technical fibers, and better dyes and chemicals. Domestic players know this — several major groups (Envoy Textiles, DBL Group, Saiham Group, Viyellatex) have invested heavily in their own spinning and fabric capacity in recent years specifically to reduce import dependency. That's real progress, but it hasn't closed the gap, and it's concentrated in basic cotton-rich categories rather than the technical/synthetic fiber space where the weakness is most severe.

### Where the actual opening is — not where the headline gap is

It would be a mistake to read "80-90% of woven fabric is imported" as "any woven fabric supplier can walk in." China already holds the largest share of that import market, and India dominates yarn on logistics rather than needing to be beaten on price. The real opening is specific, not general:

- **Technical and synthetic fiber/yarn** — where domestic capacity is weakest and demand is growing fastest, as factories chase higher-value activewear and performance categories
- **Specialty and certified inputs** — organic cotton with a genuine GOTS chain of custody, recycled-content yarn with GRS certification, or dyes/chemicals tied to a specific compliance requirement a buyer demands
- **Factories actively diversifying away from single-country dependency** — a real, current motivation given how concentrated China's share of fabric imports already is
- **Categories where Bangladesh's own expansion has stalled** — woven fabric specifically, where local investment has lagged knitwear by a wide margin
