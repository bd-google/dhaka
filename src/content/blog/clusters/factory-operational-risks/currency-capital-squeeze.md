### Why this hits raw material purchasing specifically

Bangladesh imports the large majority of its cotton and a significant share of yarn and fabric (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap piece</a>), meaning these purchases are dollar-denominated. When the taka depreciates, a factory's taka-based capital buys fewer dollars, and therefore less raw material, than it did before — industry estimates put the recent effective working-capital squeeze from this at around 35%.

### Why factories respond by cutting raw material imports

Reporting describes manufacturers responding to this pressure by sharply reducing raw material imports — a rational response to capital constraints, but one that directly disrupts inventory cycles and production planning, including for orders already committed to buyers before the squeeze intensified.

### What this means practically

This is part of why a factory might request payment terms or timeline adjustments that weren't part of the original agreement — not necessarily bad faith, but a real capital constraint working through the supply chain. Understanding this context is useful when a factory raises a cost or timing concern mid-order, and it's a reasonable factor to ask about directly when evaluating a factory's current financial footing before placing a large order.
