### How this actually unfolds

Industry reporting has described a specific, recurring failure pattern: an initial shortage of orders (or a period of reduced revenue) evolves into a working capital crisis, where the factory no longer has the financial standing to open the letters of credit needed to import fabric, yarn, or other raw material — and once that supply is cut off, production stops regardless of how capable the factory otherwise is.

### Why this is structurally fragile across the industry

Reporting on the sector notes that many textile firms rely on bank borrowing for more than 80% of their total funding, with owners contributing a much smaller equity share — a debt-heavy structure that becomes increasingly difficult to sustain as interest rates rise or as currency depreciation erodes the value of existing working capital, which has been estimated to have cut entrepreneurs' effective working capital by around a third during the recent taka depreciation.

### What this means for a buyer partway through a relationship with a factory

A factory that starts requesting unusual payment terms, delaying raw material procurement, or communicating less consistently than before may be signaling financial strain before it becomes an outright closure — worth treating as a signal to check in more directly, not just an administrative annoyance, particularly for a factory you're newer to or haven't worked with through a full order cycle before.
