### How this spiral actually works

A factory facing reduced order volume — from global demand softness, buyers shifting sourcing elsewhere, or broader economic pressure — faces a difficult choice: accept lower-margin orders to keep the factory running, or scale back and risk losing skilled workers who leave for more stable employment elsewhere. Reporting on recent factory closures specifically identifies insufficient work orders as the single largest documented cause, ahead of financial strain, labor unrest, or infrastructure issues combined.

### Why "cheap and available" can be a warning sign, not a win

A factory offering unusually aggressive pricing during a period of documented industry-wide order shortage may be pricing below sustainable cost just to keep lines running — the same dynamic that, left unaddressed, contributes to the working capital crisis and eventual closure pattern covered elsewhere in this series. An attractive quote from a factory in this position carries more downstream relationship risk than the same quote from a factory operating at healthy, sustainable volume.

### What this means practically

Asking a factory directly about their current order book and capacity utilization isn't an unusual or invasive question in the current environment — a factory confident in its position will generally answer it. A vague or evasive response to a direct question about current business volume is worth treating as a signal, particularly for a longer-term or higher-value relationship.
