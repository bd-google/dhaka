### Why the established clusters aren't automatically the safer choice

Dhaka, Gazipur, and Narayanganj carry decades of institutional garment manufacturing experience, dense supplier networks, and easier logistics to Chittagong port. They're also the same zones bearing the brunt of the current gas and electricity load-shedding crisis (see our <a href="/blog/factory-operational-risks">operational risks pillar</a>) — concentration of industry in these zones means concentration of infrastructure strain there too.

### What a newer or less-established zone can actually offer

Facilities in newer economic zones or EPZs sometimes have more modern infrastructure design and, in some cases, more reliable dedicated power arrangements specifically because they were built more recently with current standards in mind — though this isn't universal, and some newer facilities face the opposite problem of still waiting on basic utility connections (see our <a href="/blog/gas-connection-delays">gas connection delays piece</a>).

### The trade-off in plain terms

Established clusters offer proven supplier networks and logistics convenience, at the cost of sharing in that region's specific infrastructure strain. Newer zones can offer better-designed individual facilities, at the cost of a less mature local supplier ecosystem and less certainty about that specific facility's own utility situation. Neither is a universally correct choice — it depends on what you're actually optimizing for on a given order.

### What this means practically

Asking a factory directly about its specific power/gas situation and its actual location relative to the broader industrial cluster — rather than assuming "established zone" automatically means "more reliable" — is a more useful question than the zone name alone.
