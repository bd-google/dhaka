### The situation as industry leaders have described it

Bangladeshi manufacturers' association leaders have specifically noted that production costs have climbed at least 20% from higher fuel prices, transport costs, and wage increases — while many factories can't pass these costs on to buyers who locked in pricing before the current pressure intensified. The result is factories absorbing real cost increases on already-committed orders.

### Why this matters to a buyer, not just a factory

A factory absorbing unsustainable losses on your order isn't a stable long-term situation — it's the kind of pressure that contributes to the working capital crises and closures covered elsewhere in this series. A buyer benefiting short-term from a factory eating cost increases is taking on longer-term relationship risk if that factory's financial position keeps deteriorating.

### What a reasonable response looks like

For orders placed before the current energy crisis intensified, being open to a conversation about reasonable cost adjustment — particularly for an established, valued factory relationship — is a more sustainable approach than insisting on original pricing regardless of documented, verifiable cost pressure on the factory's side. This isn't about accepting every cost increase claim uncritically, but about recognizing the current environment makes some of these claims genuinely well-founded.
