### This isn't the usual "delays happen" disclaimer

Most sourcing content includes a generic line about delays being possible. This is more specific than that, because as of mid-2026, Bangladesh's garment sector is genuinely, currently working through a serious operational crisis — and understanding what it actually is changes how you should plan a production timeline right now, not as a hypothetical.

### The core problem: gas and electricity supply, not factory competence

A gas supply disruption tied to disruption in Middle Eastern energy markets since early 2026 has cut into Bangladesh's LNG imports, and the effects have cascaded directly into industrial power supply. Reports from mid-2026 describe power cuts reaching up to five hours a day in some industrial zones (Gazipur and Chattogram specifically), with many textile and garment factories operating at only 40-60% of normal capacity as a direct result — not from any failure of factory management, but from an infrastructure problem well outside any individual factory's control.

One documented, named example: a factory in Kaliakair saw daily production fall from 60 tonnes to 10 tonnes because of the power shortage, with the managing director noting the factory has to run on generators and limited grid electricity mostly overnight, with production effectively stopping for much of the working day.

### Why the generator "solution" isn't really a solution

Factories facing outages fall back on diesel generators — but diesel itself has faced supply rationing tied to the same regional disruption, and prices have risen sharply on top of that. One Chattogram factory reported daily fuel costs climbing from roughly Tk19,000 to Tk40,000 as outages lengthened. Beyond direct cost, sustained generator use causes voltage fluctuations that damage sensitive machinery over time — meaning the workaround itself creates a slower-moving, secondary cost.

### The problem behind the problem: some factories can't even get connected

Separately from load shedding at operating factories, a genuinely striking data point: reporting has identified roughly Tk35,000 crore (tens of billions of taka) in private industrial investment sitting idle across Bangladesh's economic zones, because new factories are still waiting for gas connections — in some cases, years after construction completed. This isn't a delay affecting your order specifically, but it's relevant context: it shows the scale of Bangladesh's energy infrastructure strain extends well beyond temporary load shedding at existing factories.

### Why factories are closing, not just slowing down

Between August 2024 and mid-2026, 457 factories across Bangladesh's major industrial zones closed permanently — 205 due to insufficient work orders, 190 citing financial strain, and the remainder split across labor unrest, political instability, banking issues, and the gas/electricity crisis directly. A recurring pattern in the reporting: an initial order shortage often evolves into a working capital crisis, where a factory can no longer open the letters of credit needed to import raw material, which then disrupts production entirely — a slow-motion failure that can take months to become visible from outside the factory.

### What this actually means for a buyer's planning right now

- Build real timeline buffer into any current order, not the optimistic quote a factory gives under pressure to win your business
- Ask a factory directly about their current power situation and backup capacity — this is a fair, normal question given the current environment, not an unusual one
- Avoid full dependence on a single factory for a time-critical order if you can reasonably split volume, given the documented closure rate
- Understand that a factory's cost pressure right now is real and largely outside their control — useful context if a factory raises a cost or timeline concern mid-order, rather than assuming it's a negotiating tactic
