### The actual scale of this

Reporting has documented 457 factories closing permanently across seven major Bangladeshi industrial zones between August 2024 and mid-2026 — roughly 205 due to insufficient work orders, 190 citing financial strain, and the remainder tied to labor unrest, political instability, banking issues, or the gas/electricity crisis directly. This is a meaningful closure rate across a roughly 22-month window, not a handful of isolated cases.

### Why this matters even for a factory that looks stable today

A closure is rarely instant — it's usually the endpoint of a working capital crisis, order shortage, or infrastructure strain building for months beforehand (see our <a href="/blog/factory-operational-risks">pillar piece</a> for the full pattern). A factory that looks fine on your last visit can be several months into a deteriorating situation that isn't yet visible from outside.

### What this means for how you structure a sourcing relationship

For any program where continuity matters — a repeat program, not a one-off order — avoiding total dependence on a single factory is a reasonable response to this documented closure rate, not an overreaction. A second qualified factory, even one used only occasionally, is a meaningfully cheaper insurance policy than discovering your sole supplier has closed mid-season.
