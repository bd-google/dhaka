### The current number, and why it's not the whole picture

Bangladesh's garment sector minimum wage has stood at BDT 12,500 a month since a December 2023 revision — a genuinely large 56% jump from the prior BDT 8,000 rate, but one that hasn't been revised since, even as inflation has continued. Unlike many countries, Bangladesh's Minimum Wage Board reviews the garment sector wage roughly every few years, not annually — meaning long gaps between revisions followed by larger, more disruptive jumps when they do happen, rather than small predictable annual increases.

### Why real pressure for another increase is building

Labor researchers and worker advocacy groups have specifically called for a wage closer to BDT 23,000-25,000, arguing the current rate falls well short of a genuine living wage in urban areas like Dhaka, especially given how much inflation has eroded the real value of the 2023 rate since. This is a documented, ongoing point of tension, not a settled issue — and a similarly sized jump to the 2023 revision remains a real possibility buyers should plan around, not be surprised by.

### Why buyers absorbing this, not just factories, is the honest framing

Research comparing Bangladesh's per-unit garment pricing to other major exporting countries has found Bangladeshi manufacturers paid meaningfully less for comparable products — differences in the range of 14-26% less for equivalent product categories according to one analysis of official trade data. A future wage increase that isn't reflected in what buyers pay doesn't disappear — it becomes exactly the kind of factory-side cost pressure covered in our <a href="/blog/factory-operational-risks">operational risks series</a>, one more input into the working capital strain that's been closing factories.

### What this means for realistic costing

Building a wage-increase contingency into long-term pricing agreements, rather than treating the current rate as permanent, is a more realistic planning approach than assuming static costs indefinitely. A buyer relationship that explicitly accounts for this — rather than resisting every cost conversation as if wage increases are a factory's problem alone — tends to be a more durable one, for reasons covered in our <a href="/blog/cost-pass-through-refusal">cost pass-through piece</a>.
