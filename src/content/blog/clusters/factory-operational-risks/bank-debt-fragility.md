### The structural fragility behind a lot of the other problems in this series

Banking sector analysis of Bangladesh's textile industry has highlighted a specific structural weakness: in many firms, bank borrowing accounts for more than 80% of total funding, with the factory owner's own equity contribution making up a much smaller share, often in the 10-20% range. This debt-heavy structure works fine when orders and margins are healthy — it becomes precarious quickly when interest rates rise or when a factory hits a rough stretch of orders, cost pressure, or delayed payment from buyers.

### Why this connects directly to the working capital and closure patterns covered elsewhere

A factory this leveraged has little buffer to absorb a bad quarter — a period of reduced orders, a spike in energy costs, or a payment delay from one major buyer can cascade into loan covenant pressure, difficulty opening new LCs, and the working capital crisis pattern that's been a leading driver of recent factory closures.

### What this means when evaluating a factory for a longer-term relationship

A factory's balance sheet and debt structure aren't something a buyer typically has visibility into directly, but general signals — how long the factory has operated, whether it has diversified buyer relationships rather than dependence on one or two major clients, and how it responds to direct questions about financial stability — are reasonable proxies worth asking about before committing to a program you're depending on for the long term.
