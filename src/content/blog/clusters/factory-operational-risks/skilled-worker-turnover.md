### Why this connects to the wider crisis

Reduced order volume, irregular power supply disrupting shift schedules, and general financial strain at a factory all create real incentives for skilled operators — the workers whose experience actually drives consistent quality — to move to a more stable employer if one is available. Industry coverage of the current environment has specifically linked capacity reductions to skilled worker displacement, not just idle machinery.

### Why this shows up as a quality problem before anyone calls it a staffing problem

A newer or less experienced replacement operator produces more inconsistent stitching, seam quality, and finishing than an experienced one, even on a familiar product — meaning a factory's output quality can quietly decline during a period of worker turnover well before management frames it internally as a retention issue, and certainly before a buyer is told about it directly.

### What this means for ongoing quality monitoring

A factory that performed reliably on your last few orders isn't guaranteed to perform identically on the next one if the underlying workforce has changed — a reasonable argument for continued in-line quality checkpoints on a repeat program, rather than assuming past performance is a permanent guarantee, particularly during a period of documented industry-wide instability.
