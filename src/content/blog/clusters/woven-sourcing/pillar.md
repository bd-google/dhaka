### Why woven is a genuinely different sourcing conversation than knitwear

Woven garments — shirts, trousers, jackets, workwear — aren't just "less stretchy t-shirts." The construction is different (cut-and-sew from flat woven fabric rather than knit-to-shape), the fabric supply chain is different, and critically, **Bangladesh's fabric self-sufficiency is much weaker for woven than for knit.** Where domestic mills cover the large majority of knit fabric demand, woven fabric remains heavily import-dependent (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap breakdown</a> for the full picture). That single fact changes lead time, cost sensitivity to currency and shipping conditions, and which factories are actually well set up for it.

### Woven is Bangladesh's biggest single category for the US market specifically

This is a genuinely important, underappreciated fact for buyers deciding where to source: the US has long been the largest single destination for Bangladesh's woven exports, historically taking close to half of total woven shipments — while the EU has been the dominant market for Bangladesh's knitwear. If you're a US-based buyer, woven is arguably the category Bangladesh's industry has built the deepest specific experience serving your market in.

### The fabrics that define this category

- **Twill** — a diagonal-weave fabric common in chinos and workwear
- **Poplin** — the tight, plain-weave fabric standard for dress and casual shirts
- **Canvas** — heavier, common in outerwear and workwear
- **Flannel** — brushed, warmer-weight, common in casual and seasonal shirts
- **Denim** — technically woven, but different enough in supply chain (see our <a href="/blog/denim-sourcing">denim sourcing piece</a>) that it's worth treating separately

### Composite vs. woven-specialist factories

Bangladesh's factory landscape breaks down roughly into composite factories (handling fabric through finished garment under one roof), knit-specialist factories (jersey, rib, fleece), and woven-focused factories working specifically with structured fabrics like twill, poplin, and canvas. A factory that's excellent at t-shirts isn't automatically the right choice for a tailored shirt program — construction quality on woven (collar structure, placket precision, buttonhole finishing) is a different skill set than knit sewing, and it's worth asking directly about prior woven-specific experience rather than assuming general garment capability transfers.

### Realistic MOQ and lead time

Woven MOQ commonly runs around 1,000 pieces per style for bottoms (trousers, shorts) and closer to 1,200 for shirts and tops at established factories — in the same general range as knitwear, though the fabric-import dependency means lead time can be more sensitive to shipping and customs conditions than a program built on domestically-produced knit fabric. Development time (pattern, first sample) for woven items is also often longer than for basic knit items — budget more calendar time for the sampling stage specifically.
