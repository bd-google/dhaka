### Why "does it have compliance certification" is the wrong question

A lot of first-time buyers ask a factory "are you compliance certified?" as if it's one thing. It isn't. BSCI, WRAP, and Sedex/SMETA are three different systems, run by three different organizations, checking overlapping but not identical things, and favored by different buyer markets. Asking the wrong one — or accepting whichever certificate a factory happens to hand you — is how buyers end up compliant on paper for a market they're not even selling into.

### The three you'll actually run into in Bangladesh

**BSCI (Business Social Compliance Initiative)**, run by amfori, is the one you'll see most often if you're selling into the EU — its member base is heavily German and broader-European retail (the kind of company that requires it includes major EU grocery and general-merchandise chains). Technically, BSCI isn't a certification at all — it's a monitoring and improvement framework. Audits result in a letter grade, A through E, with C typically treated as a passing grade by most member buyers. Importantly, a factory can't just sign up for BSCI on its own — it has to be invited in by a buyer who's already a BSCI member.

**WRAP (Worldwide Responsible Accredited Production)** is the one most associated with US buyers, and it's specific to the sewn-products sector — apparel, textiles, footwear, leather. Unlike BSCI, WRAP is a genuine factory-level certification against a defined set of 12 principles, and it exists independently of any single buyer relationship.

**Sedex / SMETA** is the one you'll see most with UK retailers. Like BSCI, it's a methodology rather than a pass/fail certification — audits are scored against the ETI (Ethical Trading Initiative) base code, but there's no letter grade. The full audit report goes to the buyer, and the buyer decides what's acceptable. Sedex audits also come in different depths ("2-pillar" vs. "4-pillar," the latter adding environmental and business-ethics scope), so two factories both holding "a Sedex audit" may have been checked against meaningfully different scopes.

### What none of these check — and why that matters more than people realize

Here's the part that gets skipped in most explainers, and it matters a great deal in Bangladesh specifically: **BSCI, WRAP, and Sedex audit labor conditions and management systems — not building structural integrity or fire safety.** This isn't a theoretical gap. Two factories operating inside the Rana Plaza building had passed BSCI audits before the building's collapse; the audit body itself later acknowledged its auditors weren't assessing structural safety and that this sits outside the framework's scope. Structural and fire safety in Bangladesh is a separate track entirely, historically run through the Bangladesh Accord and now continued under the RMG Sustainability Council (RSC). A factory can hold a clean BSCI or WRAP report and still have an unresolved RSC finding, or vice versa. If building safety matters to your due diligence — and it should — you check it separately, not by inference from a social-compliance certificate.

### Why factories often hold more than one

Bigger, established Bangladesh factories frequently carry two or three of these simultaneously, because they sell into multiple markets from the same production lines. That's not a sign of over-certifying — a serious composite factory selling to both a German retailer and a US brand needs both BSCI and WRAP on file, since neither buyer's compliance team will accept the other's audit as a substitute.

### What to actually verify, not just ask for

A certificate PDF handed to you in an email isn't proof of anything current. Certificates lapse, get shared informally, or apply to one unit within a larger factory group while your order runs in a different unit. What actually confirms status:

- Checking the certification body's own database where one exists, rather than relying on a forwarded PDF
- Confirming which specific factory/production unit the certificate covers — large groups often have multiple registered units, only some of which are certified
- Asking for the audit date and scope (2-pillar vs. 4-pillar for Sedex, for example), not just "do you have it"
