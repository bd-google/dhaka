### Denim is a different sourcing conversation than basics

Sourcing denim from Bangladesh isn't really the same conversation as sourcing t-shirts. The fabric mill matters, the garment factory matters, but the **wash house** is often what actually determines the final look, hand-feel, and consistency of a pair of jeans — and it's the stage first-time buyers think about least.

### Where the denim cluster actually is

**Narayanganj** is Bangladesh's denim capital, in the same way it's the historical knitwear hub — but for denim specifically, it's where the country's laundries and wash houses concentrate, alongside major fabric mills. If your program involves selvedge denim, raw denim, or any heavily washed finish, Narayanganj is almost always where that work happens, even if final garment assembly is elsewhere.

Named mills worth knowing if you're researching this space: **Envoy Textiles**, a pioneer of rope-dyeing technology in Bangladesh with its own spinning capacity; and integrated composite mills like **Nice Denim** in Gazipur, which run spinning through washing under one roof. Scale here is real — Bangladesh's denim mills collectively produce hundreds of millions of yards of denim fabric annually.

### The finishing technology actually matters to your product

Denim's final look — the fade pattern, the whiskering, the overall hand-feel — comes from finishing, not just the base fabric. Bangladesh's tier-one wash houses have moved well past manual stone washing:

- **Laser distressing** — creates repeatable fade and whisker patterns without physical abrasion damaging fiber strength as much as stone washing does
- **Ozone washing** — bleaches and fades denim using ozone gas rather than heavy chemical bleach, with a meaningfully lower water footprint
- **E-flow / nano-bubble washing** — applies dye and finishing chemistry using a foam or nano-bubble medium instead of large volumes of water

This isn't just a sustainability talking point — it's also why finish consistency across a large repeat order has genuinely improved at established wash houses. Manual stone washing has more batch-to-batch variance almost by nature; these technologies are more repeatable at scale, which matters if you're running the same wash across a 20,000-piece reorder six months apart.

### Realistic scale, MOQ, and lead time for denim specifically

Bangladesh's overall MOQ pattern applies here too — roughly 1,000+ pieces per style per color at small-to-mid factories, more at tier-one plants — but denim carries an extra wrinkle: **fabric minimums for custom-dyed or custom-woven denim often run around 3,000 meters**, which is where buyers get caught off guard, since that's a separate commitment from the garment piece count.

Lead time also runs longer than knitwear. Where a knit program might complete in 12-18 weeks, denim and complex woven programs commonly run **90 to 130 days** end-to-end once you account for fabric production, garment assembly, and the washing/finishing stage — washing alone isn't instantaneous, and a wash house's own queue affects your calendar as much as the garment factory's does.

### What to actually check before committing

- Whether the wash house is the factory's own, in-house facility or a separate subcontracted plant — subcontracted washing is a common source of unexplained delay and finish inconsistency
- Which specific finishing technologies the wash house runs — "we do stone wash" and "we run laser and ozone" are different capability tiers with different consistency profiles
- The fabric mill's dye-lot consistency track record, especially for a color or wash you plan to reorder later in the season
