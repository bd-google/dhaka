### Kidswear isn't just "smaller versions of adult clothing"

It's tempting to treat children's clothing as a simple size-down of an adult program, but the compliance requirements are genuinely different, not just scaled — and getting this wrong doesn't just mean a quality complaint, it can mean a product recall or an import rejection.

### The safety rules that only apply to children's clothing

**Drawstrings** are the single most specific, most frequently violated rule. Under CPSIA (US), drawstrings are prohibited entirely in the hood and neck area of children's tops and jackets, sizes 2T through 12 — a strangulation-risk rule with no equivalent for adult clothing. Waist drawstrings are permitted but must be reinforced with bar-tacking and kept within a specified length. The EU's equivalent is EN 14682, covering similar drawstring safety concerns with its own specific limits. A factory experienced in adult wovens but new to kidswear can easily miss this if it isn't explicitly called out in your tech pack.

**Needle and metal detection** is close to universal as a required QC step for children's apparel, and it isn't standard practice for most adult garment lines. Every finished piece gets passed through a metal detector specifically to catch broken needles or metal fragments — a real risk given how many needle passes go into a small garment, and a real hazard for a small child. If you're evaluating a factory that primarily makes adult clothing and is now pitching kidswear capability, ask directly whether they run needle detection as standard, not as a special request.

**Chemical and lead limits** are stricter than general apparel chemical-safety standards, covering lead content, phthalates, and similar substances, with jurisdiction-specific rules layered on top (California's Proposition 65 adds its own restrictions beyond the federal CPSIA baseline, for example).

**Formal documentation** goes further than a standard commercial invoice. US-bound children's apparel needs a Children's Product Certificate (CPC) — a self-issued but legally binding document affirming compliance, based on actual third-party lab test results — plus a CPSIA tracking label identifying where and when the item was produced. This paperwork doesn't exist for adult clothing in the same form, and a factory unfamiliar with kidswear exports may not have a process for producing it correctly on the first attempt.

### What this means for choosing a factory

The practical implication is straightforward: a factory's general garment-making capability doesn't automatically transfer to kidswear compliance. The right question isn't "can you make a small t-shirt" — it's whether the factory has a working process for drawstring compliance review, runs needle detection as standard, and can produce the actual certification paperwork your target market requires, not just a finished garment that looks right.

### MOQ and lead time

Bangladesh's general MOQ pattern (roughly 1,000+ pieces per style/color at established factories) applies to kidswear as it does elsewhere, though some factories with dedicated kidswear lines offer more flexibility for brands testing a new category. Lead time is comparable to standard woven/knit programs, with the compliance documentation step (lab testing, CPC preparation) adding real calendar time on top of production if it isn't started in parallel with sampling.
