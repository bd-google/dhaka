### The Production "Black Box"

Once you approve the pre-production sample, your order enters the production phase. For many foreign buyers, this is a "black box." You receive weekly emails saying "Everything is on track," only to find out on the shipping date that the fabric was delayed or the sewing line is only 50% finished.

Monitoring production from afar is impossible. You need a system that provides real-time, ground-level data.

### Step 1: Verify Raw Material Arrival

Production cannot start without fabric and trims. The most common cause of delay in Bangladesh is a late shipment of yarn or fabric from the mill.

You must ask the factory for the **Material Arrival Report**. Don't just take their word; ask for photos of the fabric rolls in their warehouse. If the fabric is ten days late, your delivery will be at least ten days late. Knowing this early allows you to adjust your marketing and shipping plans.

### Step 2: The Daily Output Report (DOR)

A professional factory tracks how many pieces are cut, sewn, and finished every day. Ask for the **Daily Output Report** for your specific lines.

If the factory promised an output of 800 pieces per day but the report shows 400, you have a problem. By spotting this in the first week of production, you can push the factory to add more lines or dedicated staff to catch up before the deadline.

### Step 3: Independent Inline Inspections

An "inline" inspection happens when about 20% of the order is sewn. This is your early warning system. An independent inspector (like Checkloom) visits the sewing floor to check the construction. They verify that the workers are following the approved PP sample. Catching a mistake at 20% is a minor fix; catching it at 100% is a disaster.

### Conclusion

Monitoring production is about **transparency**. It’s about moving from "hope" to "data." By requiring evidence of material arrival, tracking daily output, and conducting independent spot checks, you ensure that your production remains on schedule and meets your quality standards.
