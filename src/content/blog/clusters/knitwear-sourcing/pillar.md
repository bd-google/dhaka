### Why knitwear is Bangladesh's strongest category

If there's one product type Bangladesh is genuinely built for, it's knitwear. Cotton knit products — t-shirts, polos, hoodies, fleece — make up the majority of the country's garment export value, and that's not an accident of history. Knitwear factories here are usually vertically integrated: yarn spinning, circular knitting, dyeing, cutting, and sewing often happen within the same group of companies, sometimes the same complex. That matters practically, not just as a talking point — it means less time waiting on outside suppliers for fabric, and tighter shade-to-shade consistency across a large run with multiple colorways.

### Where the factories actually are

Two industrial clusters carry most of the country's knit production, and they're not interchangeable:

**Narayanganj** is the older, deeper knitwear hub — sometimes called Bangladesh's original textile town. It sits on the Shitalakshya River just south of Dhaka, and river access historically supported the area's dyeing and finishing plants. This is where you'll find the highest concentration of composite knit factories with decades of institutional knowledge in cotton knit specifically.

**Gazipur**, north of Dhaka, has grown into the larger, more industrially modern cluster — bigger vertical setups, more solar-powered and LEED-certified facilities, and generally more capacity for very large seasonal volume.

Neither is objectively "better." A smaller premium brand doing detailed, lower-volume knitwear may fit better with an established Narayanganj house; a large basics program at serious volume often makes more sense in Gazipur. This is exactly the kind of decision that's hard to make correctly from a video call, and it's a large part of what a factory visit is actually for.

### What "knitwear" covers, technically

Circular knitting machines (common brands include Mayer & Cie and Santoni) produce several distinct fabric types that behave differently and price differently:

- **Jersey** — the basic single-knit fabric behind most t-shirts
- **Rib** — used for collars, cuffs, and stretch panels
- **Interlock** — a heavier, more stable double-knit, common in polos and higher-end tees
- **Fleece / French terry** — used for hoodies and sweatshirts, typically ranging from around 280 GSM (French terry, lighter) up to 400 GSM (heavyweight fleece)

Knowing which of these your product actually needs — not just "cotton knit" as a generic spec — is one of the fastest ways to avoid a miscommunication with a factory that assumed something different from your tech pack.

### Realistic MOQ and lead time for knitwear specifically

Knitwear MOQs tend to run lower than woven garments, precisely because the fabric is usually made in-house rather than imported — a factory isn't stuck committing to a large imported-fabric minimum just to run your order. As a working range: **300 to 1,200 pieces per style per color** at most established factories, with some flexibility downward for development-stage brands at a higher unit price.

Lead time is where first-time buyers most often miscalculate. Production itself — cutting, sewing, finishing — is only part of the timeline. Yarn/fabric lead time, sampling rounds, approvals, and simply waiting for an open production slot all stack on top of that. A realistic budget for a first order with a new factory is **12 to 18 weeks end-to-end**, not the 4-6 weeks a factory might quote you if you only ask about "production time."

### What to actually verify before committing

Beyond the standard trade license and BIN checks, knitwear-specific things worth confirming directly (not just taking on faith from a sales deck):

- Which specific machines and gauge ranges the factory runs — this determines what GSM and stitch density they can actually produce consistently
- Whether dyeing and finishing happen in-house or are subcontracted out — subcontracted dyeing is a common, quiet source of shade inconsistency and delay
- Compliance certifications relevant to your buyer (BSCI, WRAP, GOTS, OEKO-TEX, SEDEX) — and whether those certifications cover the specific unit that would run your order, not just the parent group
