### The unglamorous bottleneck nobody warns you about

A fabric or trim supplier trying to break into Bangladesh usually focuses their energy on finding the right factories and making the right pitch. The part that actually derails momentum is more mundane: getting a physical sample into the country, into the right hands, without it sitting in a customs queue for three weeks while your factory conversation goes cold.

### What actually slows a sample shipment down

Customs doesn't wave through small parcels just because they're labeled "sample." A commercial invoice with vague or missing detail, or a declared value that looks understated to avoid duty, are the two most common reasons a small shipment gets flagged for manual review — and manual review is where the real delay happens. This applies even to something as small as a swatch card or a spool of thread; value and correct documentation matter more than size.

### Why parallel sampling needs coordination, not just speed

If you're sampling multiple factories at once — which you should be, for a fair comparison — the real risk isn't the shipping time, it's losing track of which factory has which version of your sample, and which feedback belongs to which conversation. A coordinated distribution point that receives your shipment once and manages onward delivery and tracking to each shortlisted factory keeps this organized in a way that mailing separately to five factories individually usually doesn't.
