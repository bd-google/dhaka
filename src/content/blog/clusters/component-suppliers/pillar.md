### Most "sell to Bangladesh" advice quietly assumes you make machinery

Search for guidance on becoming a foreign supplier to Bangladesh's garment industry, and almost everything you find is written for machinery companies — knitting machines, laser cutters, dyeing equipment. That's a real and valid market, but it's not the only one, and it's not even the biggest one by number of companies. Anyone supplying **needles, industrial thread, dyes and chemicals, zippers, buttons, labels, elastic, interlining, packaging, or testing equipment** is just as much a "Bangladesh supplier" as a machinery manufacturer — and this category gets almost no dedicated guidance.

### The scale here is real, and mostly locally supplied

Bangladesh's garment accessories and packaging sector is large in its own right — recent figures put the sector's exports (deemed and direct) at around $7.48 billion in a single fiscal year, on top of the country's roughly $39 billion in garment exports overall. Most of that demand is currently met by well-established domestic manufacturers — companies that make buttons, zippers, labels, and packaging locally, at scale, and sell directly to nearby factories.

That's worth being honest about: if you make a generic button or a standard poly bag, you're competing with domestic suppliers who have a real logistics and pricing advantage. The genuine opening for a foreign component supplier is almost always in something more specific — a specialty industrial needle brand favored for certain machine types, a technical thread with performance properties local mills don't produce, a dye or chemical formulation tied to a compliance certification (GOTS, OEKO-TEX) that requires a specific certified input, or lab/testing equipment that isn't manufactured domestically at all.

### What this actually looks like in practice — using needles as the example

Take sewing machine needles specifically, since it's about as narrow and unglamorous an example as this industry has, and it illustrates the pattern for any component. A specialty needle brand doesn't need to out-compete every local needle distributor — it needs to find the specific factories running the specific machine types where that needle brand's technical spec matters (heavy denim seams, high-speed overlock work, technical/compression fabric), and get in front of the buyers and maintenance teams making that purchasing decision. The exact same logic applies whether the product is a thread brand, a dye chemical, a testing instrument, or a machine spare part — the entry strategy is about finding where your specific spec matters, not about being cheaper than the general local supply.

### What "doing business in Bangladesh" can mean, at different levels of commitment

This isn't one path — it scales with how much presence you actually want:

- **Selling through a local agent or distributor** — the lowest-commitment option, no Bangladeshi legal entity required, useful for testing demand before committing further
- **Direct client relationships without a local entity** — viable at lower volumes, though slower without someone on the ground building relationships
- **A registered liaison office or branch (via BIDA)** — for suppliers ready to commit to an ongoing presence, our <a href="/services/suppliers/bida-registration">BIDA registration service</a> covers this
- **Local hiring** — a technician, sales representative, or office staff based in Bangladesh, which becomes relevant once you have real, ongoing volume to support
