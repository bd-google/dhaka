### This is informational, not legal advice for your specific contract

Before anything else: this is general information about how dispute resolution typically works in Bangladesh-connected commercial contracts, not a substitute for a lawyer reviewing your actual agreement. The stakes in a real dispute are too high to rely on a general overview alone — treat this as background for a conversation with legal counsel, not as the final word.

### Why arbitration, not court, is the default expectation

Bangladesh's court system is widely described, including by international legal analysis, as overburdened, with commercial litigation often taking a long time to resolve. This is the main reason serious cross-border contracts involving a Bangladeshi party typically specify arbitration rather than court litigation as the dispute resolution method — it's faster, more private, and gives parties real control over who decides the dispute and under what rules.

### The legal foundation, in plain terms

Bangladesh's Arbitration Act 2001 governs both domestic and international arbitration, and is closely modeled on the UNCITRAL Model Law — the internationally recognized framework used in arbitration-friendly jurisdictions worldwide. Bangladesh has also been a party to the New York Convention since 1992, which means arbitral awards made outside Bangladesh are generally enforceable there, and appellate courts have shown an increasingly supportive, pro-enforcement stance in practice — a meaningfully different picture than trying to enforce a foreign court judgment directly.

### Where arbitration actually happens

Bangladesh has its own arbitration institution — the Bangladesh International Arbitration Centre (BIAC), established in 2011 — which handles both domestic and international cases with its own modern rules. For cross-border contracts, parties frequently choose a foreign seat instead: the Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC) is a common choice given geographic proximity and Singapore's reputation for neutral, efficient proceedings, while contracts involving Chinese counterparties increasingly specify CIETAC given the growing volume of China-Bangladesh trade.

### Why the arbitration clause itself is the part that actually matters

A vague or poorly drafted arbitration clause is one of the most common, and most avoidable, sources of complication in a real dispute. A properly drafted clause specifies: the seat (which country's arbitration law applies procedurally), the governing institution and rules (BIAC, ICC, SIAC, or similar), the number of arbitrators (an odd number, commonly one or three), and the language of proceedings. Increasingly common practice, especially for relationships buyers and suppliers want to preserve rather than end, is a multi-tiered clause: an initial requirement to attempt resolution through senior management discussion, then mediation, and only then arbitration if earlier stages don't resolve things — giving the relationship a real chance to survive a disagreement before escalating to a binding, adversarial process.

### What this means practically for your contracts

Confirming your sourcing or supply agreements have a clear, specific arbitration clause — not just a generic one copied from a template without review — is a reasonable thing to raise with legal counsel before a dispute happens, not after. Once an actual disagreement is underway, an ambiguous clause becomes a second dispute layered on top of the original one.
