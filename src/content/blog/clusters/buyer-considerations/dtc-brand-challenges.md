### Why the standard factory model doesn't automatically fit

Most established Bangladesh factories built their operating model around large retailers: big volume, longer lead times, fewer style changes per season. A DTC brand testing products based on real-time sales data wants the opposite — smaller initial batches, faster reorder turnaround when something sells well, and more frequent style/colorway changes — a genuinely different rhythm than a factory optimized for large seasonal bulk runs.

### Where this actually causes friction

A factory used to large, infrequent orders may treat a DTC brand's smaller, more frequent orders as a lower priority relative to a bulk buyer's production slot (see our <a href="/blog/small-slot-ordering">small-slot ordering piece</a>), and may not have the sample-and-reorder-fast workflow a DTC brand actually needs to capitalize on a product that's selling well in real time.

### What actually works better

Specifically seeking out factories that already work with other DTC or smaller-batch brands, rather than a large composite factory built around bulk retail — see our <a href="/blog/low-moq">low-MOQ piece</a> for factories genuinely set up for this volume pattern. Being upfront with a factory from the start about your actual ordering rhythm (smaller, more frequent, reorder-driven) rather than assuming any capable factory will naturally adapt to it.
