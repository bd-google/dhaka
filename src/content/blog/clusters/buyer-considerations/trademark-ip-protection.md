### Why this is easy to overlook

A foreign brand's trademark protection at home doesn't automatically extend to Bangladesh — trademark rights are generally territorial, meaning separate registration is needed in each country where you want enforceable protection, including the country where your goods are actually manufactured.

### The actual process, briefly

Foreign applicants must appoint a local legal representative or trademark agent to file with Bangladesh's Department of Patents, Designs and Trademarks (DPDT) — you can't file directly without one. Initial examination typically takes 12-14 months, and if the mark clears examination and opposition, registration is valid for 10 years and renewable indefinitely. Filing fees run around BDT 3,500 per product/service class under the Nice Classification system (clothing and footwear fall under Class 25).

### The one meaningful protection that exists even without registration

Bangladesh's Trademarks Act 2009 allows the DPDT to refuse registering a mark that's identical or confusingly similar to a well-known foreign trademark, even if that foreign trademark was never registered in Bangladesh — and the well-known mark's owner can oppose or seek cancellation of a conflicting registration. This offers some protection, but it's a narrower, harder-to-invoke defense than having your own clean registration, and it doesn't help with proactive enforcement against counterfeiting (see our <a href="/blog/counterfeit-diversion-risk">counterfeit diversion piece</a>) the way registration does.

### What this means practically

If your brand name, logo, or product design is genuinely valuable and you're establishing a real manufacturing relationship in Bangladesh, registering locally — rather than relying on the "well-known mark" fallback protection — is worth raising with legal counsel specifically, particularly given the process takes over a year and is best started well before you need to actually enforce anything.
