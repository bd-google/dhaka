### How it actually happened, once

In a documented 2017 case, 26 Bangladeshi garment factories manufactured roughly Tk 600 crore worth of goods for what was presented as a British importer. The orders were placed through two local buying houses on behalf of this "buyer." The fraud surfaced only when the first shipment sat unclaimed at a UK port for over a month — because the buyer named on the paperwork wasn't real, or wasn't who they claimed to be.

### Why this works

A factory that's excited about a large order, offered attractive pricing, and working through an intermediary (a buying house) rather than the end buyer directly has fewer independent checkpoints than one dealing straightforwardly with a known, established brand. The intermediary layer is exactly where a fabricated buyer identity can hide.

### What actually protects against it

Independently verifying the end buyer's real identity and standing — not just the intermediary presenting the order — before committing significant production capacity, and treating unusually attractive pricing paired with an unfamiliar intermediary as a reason to verify harder, not a reason to move faster.
