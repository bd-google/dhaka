### This isn't a generic "be careful" warning — these are documented patterns

Sourcing content is full of vague caution about "doing your due diligence." This is meant to be more specific than that: every item below is a real, reported pattern in Bangladesh's garment trade, not a hypothetical risk category. Some hit buyers, some hit factories, and understanding which one applies to your situation is the actual point.

### Risks that mainly hit buyers

- **Fake factory websites and fronts** — copied product photos, fabricated certifications, and a physical address that doesn't match who's actually operating there. One documented case involved a full front operation using another factory's photos, caught only when a sourcing agent checked the license and address before placing an order.
- **Sample-fee and advance-payment scams** — a "supplier" that requests payment for samples or a deposit before any verifiable relationship exists, then goes quiet.
- **Bulk production that doesn't match the approved pre-production sample** — the single most common quality failure in direct sourcing without on-the-ground inspection, where measurement, finishing, or fabric substitutions only surface after goods arrive at destination.
- **Buying house impersonation** — orders placed by intermediaries who aren't who they claim to be, sometimes not even legitimate industry association members.

### Risks that mainly hit factories

- **Fake or fraudulent buyers placing large orders that are never genuine** — a documented 2017 case saw roughly Tk 600 crore in goods manufactured for a non-existent British importer, arranged through two local buying houses on behalf of a fabricated company; goods for one shipment sat unclaimed at a UK port for over a month before the fraud surfaced.
- **Real buyers defaulting or going bankrupt mid-order** — a separate documented case saw 22 exporters affected when a foreign retailer failed to pay $5 million, citing bankruptcy, for goods already shipped without an LC in place.
- **Payment coercion after goods are already produced and shipped** — documented cases of buyers demanding steep discounts (reported as high as 30%) on completed, shipped orders, effectively renegotiating after the factory has no leverage left.
- **Freight forwarder and shipping agent fraud** — a documented case affected roughly 30 exporters through a local freight forwarding agent.

### The pattern underneath all of these

Almost every item above shares one root cause: a decision — to place an order, ship goods, or release payment — made without independently verifying the other party at the point where verification would have actually mattered, not after. That's the entire logic behind checking a factory's registration before signing a contract, or checking a buyer's payment history and banking relationship before shipping without a confirmed LC.
