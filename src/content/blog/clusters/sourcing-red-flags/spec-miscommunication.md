### Why "we both speak English" doesn't fully solve this

Even in English-language correspondence, a term can carry a different default assumption on each side — "medium weight fabric," "standard finish," or "similar to the reference photo" all mean something specific to the person writing them and something else, equally specific but different, to the person reading them. This isn't a translation failure; both sides may have understood the words perfectly and still pictured a different garment.

### Why this is worse than an obvious language barrier

An obvious language barrier gets flagged and clarified, because everyone knows to double-check. A subtly different assumption inside apparently clear communication doesn't get flagged the same way — it surfaces only when the sample arrives and doesn't match what either side thought they'd agreed to.

### What actually closes this gap

A complete, specific tech pack (see our <a href="/blog/tech-pack">tech pack piece</a>) with exact measurements, construction detail, and materials — removing the vague terms that create room for differing assumptions — plus a habit of restating important specs back in your own words during any conversation, specifically to surface a mismatched assumption before it becomes a physical sample.
