### How this actually plays out

A buyer approves a pre-production (PP) sample — the reference point every unit in the bulk run should match. Weeks later, the bulk shipment arrives with measurement variation, different finishing, or fabric that doesn't match the approved sample. With no one inspecting on the ground during production, this is discovered only after the goods reach their destination — the most expensive possible point to catch it.

### Why it happens

Sometimes it's cost-cutting (a cheaper fabric batch substituted after the sample was approved on better material). Sometimes it's simply inconsistent execution across a large run without in-line quality checkpoints. Either way, the buyer's only leverage — the signed-off PP sample — only works as protection if someone is actually checking bulk production against it before shipment.

### What actually prevents this

Treating the PP sample as a binding reference document, not a formality — and having someone inspect bulk production against it before the goods ship, not after they arrive. A supplier who resists a pre-shipment inspection, or wants to skip or rush the PP sample stage entirely, is signaling something worth taking seriously before you commit to the full order.
