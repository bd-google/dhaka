### Why this happens

Blended fabric is often cheaper than the pure fiber it's meant to resemble, and the difference isn't always visible or obvious by touch to someone who hasn't specifically tested it. A factory under cost pressure on a tightly-priced order has a real incentive to substitute a blend without flagging it, especially if a buyer isn't independently testing fiber content.

### Why this is a compliance issue, not just a quality one

Most retail markets have legal requirements around accurate fiber content labeling — a mislabeled garment isn't just a disappointed customer, it's a labeling law violation the buyer, not just the factory, is exposed to once the product is on a retail shelf.

### What actually confirms this before it reaches a customer

Independent fiber content testing (a lab test, not a visual or touch check) on samples before bulk approval, especially for any order where the fabric composition specifically drives your product's marketing claims (organic cotton, specific blend ratios) — the exact claims most worth verifying independently rather than taking on trust.
