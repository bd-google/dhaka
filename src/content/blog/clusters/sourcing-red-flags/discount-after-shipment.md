### A documented, not hypothetical, pattern

Bangladeshi trade press has reported cases of buyers renegotiating price after goods were already completed and shipped — in one documented instance, a supplier described being forced to accept a 30% discount on a finished, shipped order, effectively producing the goods at a loss. This isn't a one-off; industry reporting has described a pattern of brands using their position, after production is complete, to demand discounts or delay payment on already-delivered goods.

### Why factories have so little leverage at this point

Once goods have shipped, the factory has already spent on raw materials and labor, and reversing the shipment is rarely realistic. A buyer choosing to renegotiate at this exact moment is choosing the point of maximum leverage over the factory — which is precisely why this pattern recurs.

### What actually reduces this risk

Payment terms that don't leave 100% of the value exposed until final delivery — a meaningful deposit, or a confirmed LC structure that doesn't leave full payment contingent on the buyer's goodwill after the fact. This is also a strong argument for diversifying buyer relationships rather than relying heavily on one large buyer whose negotiating leverage grows as your dependency on them does.
