### Why this happens

A factory takes on more orders than its own floor capacity can handle in the promised timeline, and quietly routes part or all of the order to a different, unapproved factory to meet the deadline — sometimes one with different (or no) compliance certification, different quality standards, or different working conditions than the one the buyer actually vetted.

### Why this matters beyond just quality risk

If your buyer's compliance program is built around a specific certified factory, and the actual production happened somewhere else entirely, any compliance claim tied to that order (BSCI, WRAP, or similar) may not reflect where the goods were actually made — a real exposure if it surfaces during an audit or a customer/regulator inquiry later.

### What actually catches this

An unannounced production visit during the run, rather than only a pre-order factory visit — subcontracting is specifically the kind of thing that's invisible from a scheduled, announced visit and much harder to hide from an unannounced one. Contract language that explicitly prohibits subcontracting without written approval also gives you a clear basis to act if it's discovered.
