### Why this isn't the same as a fake certificate

This isn't fraud in the usual sense — the certificate is real, properly issued, and unexpired. The issue is scope: large Bangladeshi factory groups often operate multiple registered production units under one company name, and a compliance certificate (BSCI, WRAP, or similar — see our <a href="/blog/compliance-certification">full breakdown</a>) is typically issued to a specific unit, not automatically to every facility the group operates.

### How this actually causes a problem

A buyer sees a valid BSCI certificate, assumes it covers "the factory," and later discovers their specific order ran at a different unit within the same group — one that wasn't the certified facility. The certificate wasn't fake; it just didn't cover where the goods were actually made.

### What actually confirms this before it's a problem

Asking specifically which production unit or facility address a certificate applies to, and confirming that's the same unit your order will physically run at — not just the parent company name on the certificate. This is worth reconfirming for any group operating multiple factories, especially if you haven't personally visited the specific unit.
