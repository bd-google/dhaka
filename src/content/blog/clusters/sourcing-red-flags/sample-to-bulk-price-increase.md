### How this plays out

An initial quote looks competitive enough to win the business. Sampling proceeds, the sample gets approved, time and effort are now invested in that specific factory relationship — and only then does a price increase appear for the bulk order, sometimes attributed to "fabric cost changes" or similar reasons that may or may not be genuine.

### Why the timing isn't a coincidence

Once a sample is approved, switching factories means starting the sampling process over with someone else — a real cost in time that a buyer, especially on a deadline, is reluctant to absorb. A factory aware of this dynamic has an incentive to quote aggressively upfront and adjust later, when the buyer's practical leverage to walk away has already dropped.

### What actually protects against this

Getting bulk pricing confirmed and, ideally, documented in writing before or alongside sample approval — not treated as a separate later conversation — so a price increase after the fact has to be justified against a specific prior commitment rather than presented as a fresh number. Comparing quotes across more than one factory before committing to sampling also reduces how much leverage any single factory has over your timeline.
