### Why this is easy to overlook

A supplier quoting in US dollars might assume currency risk is someone else's problem, since the transaction itself is dollar-denominated. But if a Bangladeshi buyer's own cost base and payment capacity is taka-denominated, currency depreciation between the order date and payment date changes the buyer's real cost of paying you — and in a market with real, documented currency pressure (see our <a href="/blog/payment-collection">payment collection piece</a>), this isn't a theoretical concern.

### How this shows up in practice

A buyer who agreed to a price weeks or months earlier may face real pressure to renegotiate, delay payment, or ask for a discount if the taka has moved significantly against the dollar in the meantime — not necessarily out of bad faith, but because their own margin has been squeezed by something outside the original agreement.

### What actually reduces this exposure

Shorter payment terms where possible, reducing the window in which currency movement can affect the deal — and factoring realistic currency volatility into your pricing and margin expectations for this specific market, rather than assuming a dollar-denominated quote fully insulates you from it.
