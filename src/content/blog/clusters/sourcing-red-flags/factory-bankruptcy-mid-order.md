### Why this is a real, not rare, risk

Bangladesh's garment sector has genuine financial pressure points — currency liquidity issues affecting factories' own working capital, thin margins on price-competitive orders, and periods of reduced order volume when global buyers pull back. A factory under real financial strain doesn't always signal it clearly before closing, and a buyer mid-production with a deposit already paid has very little recourse once that happens.

### What this actually looks like

A factory that goes quiet on communication, repeatedly pushes back delivery dates without a specific, verifiable reason, or shows signs of reduced activity (fewer workers, slower response times) partway through your production cycle — these are early signals, not just a closure announcement after the fact.

### What actually reduces this risk

Checking a factory's general financial stability and order backlog as part of initial verification, not just their registration and capability — a factory juggling far more orders than its capacity suggests, or showing signs of cash-flow strain, is a real risk signal. Structuring payment in stages tied to production milestones, rather than a large upfront deposit with nothing tied to verified progress, limits exposure if something does go wrong mid-order.
