### A documented shift in buyer behavior

Bangladesh's garment manufacturers' association has specifically flagged a trend of global buyers placing orders in smaller slots rather than bulk quantities, driven by buyers' own uncertainty about consumer demand and broader economic conditions. From the buyer's side this looks like reasonable caution; from the factory's side, it disrupts the production planning a factory relies on to run efficiently.

### Why this creates pressure that eventually reaches the buyer too

A factory planning production around large, predictable orders can run efficient, continuous production lines. Frequent smaller orders create more setup changeovers, more idle capacity between runs, and less predictable scheduling — costs that don't disappear, they show up eventually as pricing pressure, reduced flexibility on your next order, or a factory deprioritizing your smaller orders in favor of a buyer offering more volume predictability.

### What this means practically for a buyer placing smaller orders

Being upfront with a factory about your ordering pattern and volume expectations from the start, rather than starting a relationship assuming bulk-order flexibility. Factories set up for smaller, more frequent orders as their normal business model exist and are a better match than a large composite factory built around big, infrequent bulk runs.
