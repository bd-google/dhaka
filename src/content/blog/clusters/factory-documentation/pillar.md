### The order that got away for a reason that had nothing to do with your factory floor

A factory can have the machinery, the workforce, and the compliance record to run a buyer's order exactly as specified — and still lose that order to a competitor, because the paperwork made the competitor look more capable. This isn't a hypothetical; it's a specific, common, and preventable failure mode, and it happens at the documentation stage, not the production stage.

### Why this happens even to genuinely capable factories

A buyer evaluating several factories in parallel — which is standard practice, not an insult to any one factory — is largely comparing what arrives in writing: the tech pack response, the compliance paperwork, the email correspondence. If one factory's response is a quick "yes we can do this" and another's is a clear, specific breakdown confirming the exact measurements, construction detail, and fabric spec understood correctly, the second factory looks more capable — regardless of whether the actual production floors are equally competent. Buyers can't inspect your sewing line from an email; they can only evaluate what you send them.

### The three places this actually shows up

**Tech pack responses.** A response that restates the buyer's own spec back to them accurately, flags any genuine constraint clearly (not buried, not omitted), and asks specific clarifying questions where needed, reads as a factory that understood the brief. A generic "no problem, we can make this" response — even when true — doesn't demonstrate that understanding, and buyers who've been burned before specifically look for evidence of it.

**Compliance documentation.** Certificates need to be current, and specific to the exact production unit that would run the order — a certificate technically belonging to a parent group's different factory doesn't answer a buyer's actual question, and presenting it as if it does erodes trust rather than building it.

**Ongoing written communication.** Since email remains the primary channel for B2B sourcing communication in this industry, the clarity and professionalism of routine correspondence is doing more evaluation work, continuously, than most factories realize.

### This is a documentation-and-translation problem, not just a translation problem

A technically accurate translation that still doesn't answer the buyer's actual question, or arrives in a format international buyers aren't expecting, produces the same lost-order outcome as a straightforward language barrier. The fix isn't just "translate it into English" — it's presenting specs, certifications, and correspondence in the structure and level of detail a foreign buyer's own sourcing team is used to receiving from other suppliers around the world.
