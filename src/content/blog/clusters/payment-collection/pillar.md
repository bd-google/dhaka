### This isn't a rumor — it's a documented, recurring problem

If you're a foreign supplier considering Bangladesh, you should know this going in, not discover it after your first shipment: Bangladesh's banking sector has had a real, well-documented US dollar shortage since around mid-2022, and it has repeatedly caused delays in import letter of credit (LC) payments to foreign suppliers. At points, overdue LC payments across the banking sector have been reported in the hundreds of millions of dollars, with individual textile mills facing multi-million-dollar payment delays even after goods were already supplied. Bangladesh Bank has had to issue circulars explicitly telling commercial banks that delaying payments against clean, accepted bills is "unacceptable" — more than once, which tells you the problem doesn't resolve itself just because a regulator says so.

### What actually causes the delay

It isn't fraud or bad faith in most cases — it's a genuine foreign currency liquidity problem. Banks sometimes hold the taka-side funds but lack sufficient US dollar reserves to settle the foreign leg of the transaction, particularly smaller and state-owned banks. Some banks have been reported prioritizing large corporate clients' LCs over smaller importers, meaning the risk isn't distributed evenly — a supplier working with a smaller or newer Bangladeshi buyer faces more exposure than one working with an established, large exporter with strong banking relationships.

### The real costs beyond the delay itself

Even when an LC does clear, the cost structure has friction built in: confirmation charges typically run 2.5-3% annually, and discrepancy fees, amendment fees, and SWIFT charges can add up to a further 3-5% of transaction value according to industry analysis. A single missing seal or mismatched detail on a proforma invoice can trigger a discrepancy fee and a real delay — this isn't a rare edge case, it's a routine friction point in Bangladesh's LC process.

### How to structure payment terms around this risk, not just hope it doesn't happen

- **Confirmed LC, not just an issued one** — a confirming bank (typically an international bank) adds its own payment guarantee on top of the issuing bank's, which matters specifically because the risk here is a Bangladeshi bank's own dollar liquidity, not the buyer's creditworthiness
- **Partial advance payment** — even 20-30% upfront changes your exposure meaningfully on a first order with a new buyer
- **Check which bank is issuing the LC** — this sector's payment-delay risk isn't evenly distributed; the buyer's specific bank matters as much as the buyer itself
- **Build a realistic payment timeline into your production schedule** — treating "LC payment on shipment" as instant is how suppliers get caught off guard; build in a buffer
