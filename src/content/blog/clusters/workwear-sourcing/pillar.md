### The question that actually matters here isn't "can you make a jacket"

Workwear and PPE (personal protective equipment) is the one apparel category where basic sewing competence is almost beside the point. A factory can be excellent at fashion jackets and still not be equipped to produce compliant flame-resistant coveralls or high-visibility safety vests — because these products are regulated, tested categories with specific standards a general apparel factory has no particular reason to have invested in.

### The standards that actually govern this category

- **ANSI/ISEA 107 (US) and EN ISO 20471 (EU)** — govern high-visibility workwear, typically specifying Class 2 or Class 3 garments made from certified fluorescent fabric with retroreflective tape applied and tested to standard, not just any reflective-looking trim sewn on
- **EN 340** — a general baseline standard for protective workwear construction and labeling
- **Flame-resistant (FR) certification** — requires certified FR fabric sourcing and construction technique, and for some industrial applications (oil and gas, chemical processing), ATEX zone compliance specifically for explosive-atmosphere environments
- **Healthcare/hygiene-appropriate production** — scrubs, lab coats, and clinical garments need production run under hygiene-appropriate conditions, a different factory-floor consideration than standard apparel manufacturing

### Why this filters out more factories than any other category

A factory that lists "workwear" as a product category isn't necessarily certified or equipped for the regulated end of it. The realistic filter is asking directly and specifically: does the factory source certified FR or hi-vis fabric (not generic fabric that looks similar), and can they show test reports for retroreflective tape application, not just a claim that it's "reflective." This is a category where a sales deck's product photos tell you almost nothing about actual compliance capability.

### The breadth within "workwear" itself

This category spans a wide range of sub-specializations that don't all live at the same factory: industrial coveralls and boiler suits, hi-vis road and construction wear, flame-resistant garments for industrial/utility work, healthcare uniforms and scrubs, and softshell/fleece outdoor workwear. A factory network approach — different certified partner factories for different sub-categories — is common in this space specifically because the compliance requirements are genuinely different across these sub-segments.

### Realistic MOQ

Workwear MOQ runs a wider range than most categories, partly because a lot of demand comes from institutional and corporate uniform buyers rather than fashion retail. Some factories offer small-batch runs from around **100 pieces per style** for buyers testing a new uniform design, while standard bulk production sits in the same 1,000+ range as general apparel.
