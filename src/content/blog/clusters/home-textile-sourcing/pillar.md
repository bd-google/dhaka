### A smaller, more specialized factory base than garments generally

Home textiles — terry towels, bed and bath linen, kitchen linen — is a real, established Bangladesh export category, but it runs on a much smaller and more concentrated factory base than general garments: roughly 25-30 dedicated factories, compared to the thousands of factories in the broader RMG sector. Bed and bath linen makes up the largest share of this category, around 65%, with kitchen linen a smaller secondary segment.

### The honest disadvantage worth knowing upfront

This is the kind of thing a sourcing agent should tell you rather than let you discover later: cotton and yarn account for roughly 70% of the cost of producing a towel, and Bangladesh's near-total dependence on imported raw cotton (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap piece</a> for the full picture) puts it at a real cost disadvantage against Pakistan specifically in this category — Pakistan grows its own cotton, which matters more for a cotton-intensive product like towels than it does for general apparel with more varied fiber content. This has shown up concretely in past periods of currency-driven price competition, where Bangladeshi home textile exporters have felt real pressure from Pakistani pricing.

### Where Bangladesh's real strength is in this category

Despite the cotton disadvantage, Bangladesh has genuine specialist depth here — established manufacturers with decades of terry-towel-specific experience producing at real scale, and duty-free access to the EU, Canada, and Japan under preferential trade schemes (though notably not the same duty preference in the US market, which is worth factoring into landed-cost comparisons by destination).

### The niche growing faster than the rest of the category

Hospitality and institutional linen — hotels, hospitals, and similar bulk buyers — is growing at a faster rate than general retail home textile demand, and it comes with its own specific technical requirement: durability standards like chlorine-fast whites and resistance through 150 or more industrial wash cycles, which is a different and more demanding specification than a retail bath towel needs to meet. Not every home textile factory tests for or guarantees this specifically — worth asking about directly if your buyer is in the hospitality or institutional space rather than retail.

### A recent shift worth knowing about

Newer digital sourcing platforms have started lowering the traditional entry barrier for this category, with some enabling smaller batch orders (reportedly as low as around 300 units) that wouldn't have been realistic through traditional channels a few years ago — a genuinely useful development if you're a smaller brand that assumed home textiles required large-volume commitment.
