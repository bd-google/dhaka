### The gap that costs buyers the most money

Ask any experienced sourcing team what causes the most first-order problems in Bangladesh, and "incomplete tech pack" comes up more than factory quality, more than compliance, more than communication delays. It's not that factories can't handle detailed specs — Bangladesh factories work from tech packs for major global brands every day. It's that first-time buyers often don't know what a complete one actually requires, and send a photo or a rough description instead.

### What happens when a factory doesn't have one

Without a complete tech pack, a factory isn't refusing to help — it's filling in gaps with its own assumptions, and those assumptions cost you money in three specific ways:

1. **The quote comes in higher than it needs to be.** A factory that doesn't know your exact fabric weight, construction, and trim details will quote conservatively to cover the unknowns, not aggressively to win your business.
2. **The first sample doesn't match what you pictured.** This is the most common complaint from first-time buyers, and it's almost never a factory quality problem — it's a specification gap. If seam allowance, collar ratio, or fit measurements weren't stated, the factory made a reasonable guess that wasn't your guess.
3. **Disputes have nothing to point back to.** A tech pack functions as the working agreement between you and the factory. Without one, "that's not what I asked for" has no document to settle it.

### What a complete tech pack actually includes

- **Flat technical sketches** — front, back, and any detail views (not a photo of a finished garment, a technical drawing showing construction)
- **Measurements and grading** — the full size specification across your full size range, not just one sample size
- **Construction detail** — seam types, stitch types, seam allowances, and any reinforcement points
- **Bill of materials (BOM)** — every fabric, trim, label, and component itemized, including fabric composition and GSM
- **Colorways** — exact colors specified against a recognized system (Pantone TCX or similar), not "navy blue"

A "spec sheet" and a "BOM" are sometimes used loosely to mean parts of this — a complete tech pack includes both, along with the sketches and construction notes, as one document a pattern maker and sample room can execute from without asking you follow-up questions for every decision.

### A real pattern worth knowing before it happens to you

One recurring failure mode: a buyer has a tech pack, a target price, and a production window, and everything looks fine on paper — until a fabric shade variation shows up that nobody caught until the fit sample stage, alongside an MOQ mismatch nobody flagged upfront (the factory needing twice the quantity per color that the buyer expected). Neither issue was really about the tech pack's completeness — they were about what happens *after* the tech pack is sent, during supplier screening and sampling, when there's no one checking that the factory's stated capability matches the tech pack's actual requirements. A complete tech pack reduces guessing; it doesn't replace someone checking the factory can actually deliver against it.
