### The Gap Between Design and Reality

You’ve spent weeks perfecting your designs. You have high-resolution 3D renders and a beautiful tech pack. You send it out to ten factories in Bangladesh, expecting a flood of quotes. Instead, you get silence. Or, you get quotes that are double what you budgeted for.

Why? Because in the world of industrial garment manufacturing, design is only half the battle. The other half is **supply chain feasibility**. Bangladesh is a powerhouse for cotton and polyester basics, but if your design requires specialized technical fabrics, recycled blends with low MOQs, or highly complex construction at a "fast fashion" price point, you are fighting an uphill battle.

### The Fabric MOQ Trap

The most common reason for a project to fail in Bangladesh isn't the factory's skill—it's the fabric MOQ. Most mills in Bangladesh require at least 500kg to 1,000kg of fabric for a custom dye run. For a standard t-shirt, 1,000kg of fabric makes roughly 3,000 to 4,000 garments.

If you only want 200 pieces per color, but you want a custom Pantone-matched shade in a specific weight, the factory has two choices:
1. Reject the order.
2. Buy the minimum 1,000kg of fabric and charge you for the 900kg they won't use.

This is why many small brands feel "ghosted" by factories. The factory knows the fabric spec is impossible for your volume, and they don't want to spend time explaining the economics of a textile mill.

### Cotton vs. Technical Synthetics

Bangladesh is the king of cotton. If your brand is built on jersey, fleece, piqué, or twill, you are in the right place. The local ecosystem is optimized for these materials. However, if you are designing high-performance activewear with 4-way stretch, moisture-wicking coatings, and bonded seams, you need to be careful.

Most high-performance synthetic fabrics are still imported into Bangladesh from China, Taiwan, or South Korea. This adds 3 to 4 weeks to your lead time and subjects you to the MOQs of the international mills. If you are a startup, it is often better to use "locally available" fabrics for your first collection to keep costs and risks manageable.

### The "Target Price" Reality Check

We often see buyers asking for "premium quality" (think Lululemon or Ralph Lauren standards) at "stock lot" prices ($2.00 for a polo shirt). This simply doesn't work.

A realistic budget in Bangladesh must account for:
* **Raw Materials**: ~50-60% of the cost.
* **CM (Cut & Make)**: The labor and overhead cost of the factory.
* **Testing & QC**: Independent lab tests for color fastness and shrinkage.
* **Logistics**: Getting the goods to the port.

If your target price is lower than the cost of the raw materials alone, no factory can help you without cutting corners on wages or safety—something that will eventually destroy your brand's reputation.

### How to do a "Spec Reality Check"

Before you send your tech pack to 20 factories, do a feasibility audit:
1. **Material Check**: Is this fabric regularly produced in Bangladesh?
2. **Volume Match**: Does my order volume justify the custom fabric requirements?
3. **Construction Check**: Does this garment require specialized machinery (e.g., seam taping, laser cutting) that only 5% of factories have?
4. **Price Benchmark**: Is my target price based on current market data or just a guess?

At ThreadBridge BD, we perform these feasibility checks for our clients every day. We tell you the truth before you waste months on samples that will never go to bulk. We help you adjust your specs—perhaps changing a custom dye to a "near-match" stock color—so that you can actually get your brand off the ground and into the hands of your customers.

### Conclusion

Bangladesh is a land of opportunity, but it rewards the well-prepared. By aligning your design specs with the reality of the local supply chain, you move from "unrealistic dreamer" to "serious buyer" in the eyes of the factory.
