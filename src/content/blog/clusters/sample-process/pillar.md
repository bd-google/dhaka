### The Sampling Journey

The most critical phase of your production isn't the bulk sewing—it's the sampling. This is where your vision is translated into a physical reality. For many small buyers, this process can feel like a frustrating game of "lost in translation." You send a tech pack, and two weeks later, you receive something that looks like it was made for a different brand.

Understanding the standard stages of sampling in Bangladesh will help you manage your timeline and your expectations.

### Stage 1: The Proto Sample (The "First Draft")

The Proto sample is about silhouette and construction. Factories will often use "available" fabric (similar weight but maybe a different color) and generic trims to make this sample. Don't panic if the color is wrong; focus on the measurements and the way the garment hangs. This is the time to make big changes to the design.

### Stage 2: The Fit Sample (The "Refinement")

Once the Proto is approved, the factory moves to the Fit sample. This should be made in the correct fabric (if available) or a very close substitute. This sample is used to verify the size grading. Many brands use fit models or dress forms at this stage. You are looking for comfort, movement, and ensuring the "drape" of the fabric matches your intent.

### Stage 3: The PP Sample (The "Contract")

The Pre-Production (PP) sample is the most important. It must be made in the exact fabric, with the exact trims, using the exact sewing machines that will be used for bulk. Once you sign the tag on a PP sample (the "Gold Seal"), it becomes the legal standard for your order. If the bulk production doesn't match the PP sample, you have the right to reject it.

### Common Pitfalls for Small Buyers

1. **Changing Specs Mid-Stream**: Every time you change a measurement or a pocket placement, you reset the clock. Try to finalize your design at the Proto stage.
2. **Ignoring the Wash**: If your garment is garment-dyed or enzyme-washed, the sample must undergo the same process. Shrinkage is real, and it can ruin a perfect fit if not accounted for during pattern making.
3. **Mailing Samples Too Early**: Before you ask the factory to mail a sample to you (at your expense), ask for high-resolution photos and a "measurement vs. tech pack" table. If the photos show an obvious error, have them fix it locally before it leaves Bangladesh.

### Conclusion

Sampling is a conversation between you and the pattern maker. By being clear, using photos for feedback instead of just text, and respecting the stages of the process, you can reach the PP sample stage faster and with much less stress.
