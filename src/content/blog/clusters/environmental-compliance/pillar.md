### Why "do you have an ETP" is the wrong question

Almost every factory doing dyeing, washing, or wet-processing in Bangladesh will tell you, correctly, that they have an Effluent Treatment Plant — it's been a legal requirement for textile wet-processing since 2003, with a 2008 compliance deadline. The real question isn't whether one is installed. It's whether it actually runs continuously, treating every batch of wastewater to standard, rather than sitting idle except when an inspection is expected.

### The documented scale of this exact problem

In February 2025, Bangladesh's Department of Environment issued final warning notices to 2,046 industrial factories across Dhaka, Gazipur, Narayanganj, and Narsingdi — not for lacking an ETP, but specifically for failing to keep already-installed ETPs operational at all times. This is a real, recent, large-scale enforcement action targeting exactly the gap between "has an ETP" and "actually runs it."

### Why this happens

Running an ETP continuously costs real money — electricity, chemical treatment inputs, and staff to operate it — on top of the capital cost of installing it in the first place. A factory under the financial pressure covered in our <a href="/blog/factory-operational-risks">operational risks series</a> has a real, if not admirable, incentive to run treatment only when inspection is expected rather than on every batch, treating it as a cost to minimize rather than a continuous operational requirement.

### The scale of what's actually at stake

Textile wet processing in Bangladesh has been documented as using 200-250 liters of water per kilogram of fabric — roughly five times the international best-practice benchmark of around 50 liters per kilogram. Untreated or under-treated discharge from this volume of water use has measurably degraded water quality in the rivers surrounding Dhaka specifically, which is the direct reason for the DoE's recent enforcement push.

### What actually distinguishes a genuinely compliant factory

- **A current Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC)** issued by the Department of Environment, not just an ETP installation
- **Willingness to show actual, ongoing treatment records** — not just the physical facility on a factory tour, but discharge testing data over time
- **LEED-certified or similarly audited facilities** have been shown in recent research to have measurably better ETP design and operational effectiveness than non-certified factories — a useful proxy signal, though not a substitute for checking directly
