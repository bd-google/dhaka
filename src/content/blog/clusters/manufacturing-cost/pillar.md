### There's no single answer, but there is a real range

"How much does it cost to manufacture clothing in Bangladesh?" doesn't have one number, because the honest answer depends on three things that change the price independently: what the garment is made of, how many you're ordering, and what "finished" means for your brand. But it's not a black box either. The cost structure is well understood inside the industry — it's just rarely explained to buyers before they've already signed a proforma invoice.

### The three-part cost structure

Every FOB (Free On Board) quote you get is really three numbers added together, and knowing the split tells you where to actually negotiate.

**Fabric — usually 60-70% of the total cost.** This is the largest lever by far. A basic 180 GSM combed cotton knit costs meaningfully less than a 220 GSM heavyweight or an organic/GOTS-certified equivalent. If a quote seems too good, check the fabric spec first — it's the most common place corners get cut.

**CMT (Cut, Make, Trim) — the actual labor.** For a basic t-shirt, this typically lands somewhere between $0.25 and $1.00 per piece, depending on construction complexity. A simple crew-neck tee sits at the low end; drop-shoulder, reinforced seams, or multi-panel construction push it toward the high end. This is where Bangladesh's cost advantage over China and much of Vietnam actually comes from — not from cutting corners, but from a very large, specialized RMG workforce operating at scale.

**Trims, decoration, and packaging — the line item people forget to ask about.** Basic woven labels and a polybag might add $0.10-$0.20. Custom hangtags, embossed buttons, or specialty packaging can add $0.50-$1.50. None of this is hidden exactly — it's just usually quoted separately, and buyers comparing two factory quotes sometimes don't realize they're comparing "bare garment" to "fully finished."

### Realistic price bands (FOB, per piece)

These are ranges, not quotes — your actual number depends on your factory, your tech pack, and your negotiating position. But as a sanity check against what you're being quoted:

- **Economy basic tee** (160-180 GSM, simple construction, 1-color print): roughly $2.00-$3.00
- **Standard retail tee** (180-200 GSM, branded label, 2-color print): roughly $2.50-$3.50
- **Premium/D2C tee** (200-220 GSM ring-spun, custom trims): roughly $4.00-$5.50
- **Organic/GOTS-certified**: often $6.00-$8.50, largely because of the certified fabric, not the labor
- **Basic knitwear (sweaters, fleece)**: varies widely by yarn and construction — this is worth a direct quote rather than a rule of thumb

All of these assume order volumes of 1,000+ pieces per style/color. Below that, expect a real markup — see the MOQ section below.

### Why your order size changes the math more than anything else

A factory sets up a cutting table and a sewing line the same way whether you're ordering 200 pieces or 10,000. That fixed setup cost gets spread across your order — so the smaller the order, the more of that fixed cost lands on each piece. Realistically:

- Small-to-mid factories often want 1,000+ pieces per style/color as a working minimum
- Larger, tier-one factories may not engage seriously below 5,000
- Below your factory's comfortable minimum, expect a 25-45% per-piece markup, or a straight "no"

There's a second MOQ trap that catches first-time buyers specifically: **the fabric minimum is often higher than the garment minimum.** A factory might accept a 1,000-piece garment order but need 3,000 meters of a custom-dyed fabric to run it — meaning you're actually paying for the fabric minimum whether you asked for it or not. Always confirm both numbers before signing anything, not just the piece count.

### What compliance certification actually adds — and why it's not a cost you should cut

BSCI, WRAP, GOTS, and similar certifications add a real, upfront cost — inspection fees, sometimes facility upgrades. It's tempting for a first order to look for a factory without any of it, since the quote will be lower. In practice, this is the wrong place to save money: it's the single biggest source of downstream risk (customs holds, retailer compliance rejections, reputational exposure) for what's usually a small percentage difference in unit price. If a factory's certifications don't match what your buyer/retailer requires, find out before production, not after the container ships.

### The hidden cost most buyers only discover once

Beyond fabric, CMT, and trims, there's a category of cost that rarely shows up on the first quote: documentation and port-side fees, and the cost of a factory visit or third-party inspection you didn't budget for because your first factory "seemed fine" on a video call. None of these are large individually. Together, on a first order, they're the most common reason a landed cost ends up 8-15% above what the FOB quote implied.
