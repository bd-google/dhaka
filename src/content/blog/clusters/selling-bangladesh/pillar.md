### The Two-Headed Dragon: Brands vs. Factories

If you want to sell fabric or trims in Bangladesh, you have to understand the "Nomination" system. For many large orders, the end-buyer (e.g., H&M, Zara, Walmart) specifies exactly which fabric mill the factory must use. This is called a nomination.

However, for a growing number of orders—especially in the "fast fashion" and boutique sectors—the factory is given a "Target Price" and a "Design Sketch" and is told to find the best fabric themselves. This is where the real opportunity for foreign suppliers lies. If you only talk to the brands in London, you miss 50% of the market. You need to be selling to the factory merchandisers in Dhaka and Chittagong.

### The Procurement Cycle in Dhaka

The Bangladesh garment industry works on a seasonal rhythm. If you are selling for the Autumn/Winter collection, you need to be in the factories with your samples at least six months in advance.

The process usually looks like this:
1. **Introduction**: Submitting your company profile and broad product range.
2. **Sampling**: The factory asks for specific swatches to build "proto" garments.
3. **Quoting**: Once the fabric is approved, you provide your best FOB price.
4. **L/C Opening**: The factory opens a Letter of Credit (the standard payment method in Bangladesh).
5. **Shipment**: You ship the goods to the port of Chittagong.

### The Importance of the "Shadow Lead Time"

When you calculate your lead time, don't just think about production and shipping. You must account for the "Shadow Lead Time"—the time it takes for a factory to get L/C approval from their bank and for the documents to be couriered.

In Bangladesh, banking can be slow. A 30-day production lead time can easily become a 60-day delivery time if the L/C is delayed by two weeks. Successful suppliers account for this and communicate clearly with both the factory merchandiser and the finance manager.

### Selling Machinery: The Total Cost of Ownership

If you are selling machinery, your pitch shouldn't just be about the price. Bangladesh factory owners are increasingly focused on **Efficiency** and **Compliance**.

If your machine uses 20% less water or electricity than a Chinese competitor, that is a massive selling point. Energy costs in Bangladesh are rising, and "green" factories get better rates from global brands. Your sales presentation should focus on the "Return on Investment" (ROI)—how many months it will take for the energy savings to pay for the machine.

### Conclusion

Selling into Bangladesh is a marathon, not a sprint. It requires a mix of high-level brand nominations and ground-level factory relationships. By understanding the local procurement culture and focusing on the buyer's real problems—speed and energy costs—you can build a sustainable and highly profitable export business.
