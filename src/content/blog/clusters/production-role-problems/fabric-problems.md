### Why fabric quality determines the ceiling for everything downstream

No amount of skilled sewing fixes a defective roll of fabric — fabric quality sets the ceiling for the finished garment's quality, and given fabric typically makes up 60-70% of total garment cost (see our <a href="/blog/manufacturing-cost">manufacturing cost breakdown</a>), it deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets before cutting begins.

### 1. Bowing and skewing

Bowing is when the crosswise (weft) yarns curve across the fabric width instead of running straight; skewing is when they run at an angle instead of perpendicular to the selvage. Both come from uneven tension during dyeing or finishing, and both cause twisted seams and an unbalanced finished garment — most visible on striped or patterned fabric, where the distortion is easy to see, but present on solid colors too.

### 2. Shrinkage that isn't caught before cutting

A garment can pass measurement spec before washing and fail after — fabric that wasn't properly relaxed or pre-treated shrinks unevenly once heat and moisture are introduced. This is why fabric relaxation before cutting, not just a visual roll inspection, matters specifically for cotton, rayon, viscose, and unstable-finish fabrics.

### 3. Shade variation within and across rolls

Dyeing is a batch process, and even a correct color code doesn't guarantee identical shade from roll to roll, or sometimes even within one roll — see our dedicated piece on <a href="/blog/fabric-shade-variation">fabric shade variation</a> for why this specifically causes problems across a large cutting lay or a later reorder.

### 4. Needle lines in knit fabric

A defective needle on a circular or flat knitting machine produces a visible vertical streak running the length of the fabric — a single defective needle causes an individual line, while a broader dial or cylinder problem produces a band of multiple lines. This is a machine-maintenance issue at the knitting stage, not a fabric-material problem, but it shows up as a fabric defect at inspection.

### 5. Barre (striping)

A subtle but real striping effect caused by inconsistent yarn tension during knitting — different yarn batches or slight tension variation between feeders creates faint crosswise bands, most visible in solid colors under certain lighting, and easy to miss on a quick visual check.

### 6. GSM inconsistency

Fabric weight (grams per square meter) that varies from what was specified — sometimes across a single roll, not just between different batches — affects hand-feel, drape, and durability, and isn't always visible without an actual weight check rather than a visual inspection.

### 7. Holes, snags, and runs

Caused by improper handling and storage, poor-quality yarn, or manufacturing damage — particularly common with more delicate fabrics like silk, nylon, and rayon, and often introduced during transport or warehouse handling rather than at the mill itself.

### 8. Mispicks, missing yarns, and broken ends

Construction-level weaving defects — a missing or broken yarn in the weave structure, or picks (crosswise yarns) that skip or misalign — that weaken the fabric structure and affect both appearance and durability.

### 9. Pilling tendency

Some fabric constructions and fiber blends are inherently more prone to surface pilling (small balls of fiber forming with wear and friction) than others — a defect that isn't visible on a new roll but becomes obvious after the customer wears and washes the garment a few times, by which point it's too late to fix.

### 10. Yarn contamination and slubs

Foreign fiber or irregular thickened sections (slubs) in the yarn itself show up as visible imperfections in the finished fabric — tracing back to a quality issue at the spinning stage rather than anything that happened during weaving, knitting, or finishing.

### What this means practically

Fabric defects come from spinning, knitting or weaving, dyeing, finishing, or handling — meaning inspection needs to happen at multiple points, not just a single check when the finished garment ships. Many of these defects (shrinkage, colorfastness, pilling) are specifically invisible until washing or wear, which is exactly why testing fabric behavior before bulk cutting matters more than a purely visual roll inspection.
