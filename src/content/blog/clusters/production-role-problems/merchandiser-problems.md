### Why so many "production problems" actually start here

The merchandiser's job is to sit between the buyer's requirements and the factory floor's execution — translating a tech pack into a costed, scheduled, trackable order, and coordinating sourcing, sampling, and production against a deadline. When something goes wrong later and gets labeled a "production" or "quality" problem, a real share of the time the actual root cause sits here, at the coordination layer, not on the sewing line.

### 1. Design and spec changes that don't fully reach the floor

A buyer requests a change mid-development — a measurement adjustment, a trim swap — and the merchandiser communicates it, but not every detail makes it cleanly to every department that needs to know. Cutting works from one version, finishing expects another. This is less about anyone being careless and more about how many separate handoffs a single change has to survive.

### 2. Informal communication channels losing information

A instruction given over a phone call or a quick WhatsApp message, without a written follow-up, is easy to lose or misremember days later when it actually matters — at the point of dispute, there's often no record of what was actually agreed. See our piece on <a href="/blog/spec-miscommunication">spec miscommunication</a> for how this plays out even when everyone believes they communicated clearly.

### 3. Promising a delivery date without checking real floor capacity

A merchandiser under pressure to keep a buyer happy sometimes commits to a timeline based on what the buyer wants to hear rather than a confirmed check with production planning — see our piece on <a href="/blog/capacity-overpromising">capacity overpromising</a> for how this plays out once the order is actually running.

### 4. Costing built on incomplete technical detail

A quotation prepared before every construction and trim detail is finalized risks being wrong in either direction — undercosted (squeezing margin once the real spec is locked) or overcosted (losing the order to a competitor's more accurate quote). Costing accuracy is only as good as the technical detail it's based on at the time.

### 5. Trims and accessories procurement started too late

A merchandiser juggling multiple orders can deprioritize a small trim order relative to the "big" fabric decision — and a missing button or zipper stops a finished garment just as effectively as missing fabric does. See our piece on <a href="/blog/trims-shortage-delay">trims shortage and delay</a>.

### 6. Sample approval delays that quietly eat the production calendar

Every day a sample sits waiting for buyer sign-off is a day removed from the bulk production window — but that lost time doesn't always get renegotiated into the final delivery date, leaving the factory floor to somehow make up time it never actually had.

### 7. Being the single point of contact with no backup

An order where all buyer communication and floor coordination runs through one merchandiser is fragile — if that person is unavailable, out sick, or leaves the company mid-order (merchandiser turnover is genuinely common in this industry), institutional knowledge about that specific order's history and agreements can go with them.

### 8. Getting blamed for problems that originated elsewhere

A merchandiser fields the buyer's complaint regardless of where a problem actually started — a fabric mill's late delivery, a compliance document a factory's admin team was slow to produce — meaning the person best positioned to explain what actually happened is also the one absorbing the frustration for it.

### 9. Not tracking recurring issues systematically

Without a habit of logging what actually went wrong on past orders — not just fixing the immediate problem — the same avoidable issue (a specific trim supplier's chronic lateness, a specific buyer's habit of late spec changes) repeats on the next order because nobody built a record of the pattern.

### 10. Managing too many orders in parallel without enough real bandwidth

A merchandiser stretched across more concurrent orders than can genuinely get close attention on each one is a structural setup for exactly the problems above — not because of individual failure, but because coordination quality degrades once attention is split too thin across too many simultaneous orders.
