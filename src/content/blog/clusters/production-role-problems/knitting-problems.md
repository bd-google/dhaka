### Why the knitting stage deserves its own scrutiny, separate from "fabric quality" generally

A lot of what shows up as a general fabric defect actually traces back to a specific, identifiable mechanical cause at the knitting machine itself — a worn needle, a miscalibrated cam, a tension setting pushed too tight for the yarn being used. Understanding the actual mechanical root cause matters because it tells you whether a defect is a one-off or a signal of a bigger, recurring machine problem.

### 1. Dropped stitches

Recurrent openings in one or more wales (vertical columns of loops) where the stitch simply didn't form — the most basic and visible knitting defect, and the starting point for several worse ones if it isn't caught (a dropped stitch left unaddressed can develop into a full ladder run).

### 2. Needle line

A lengthwise streak running the full length of the fabric, caused by a single needle that's misaligned, sitting too tight in its slot, or paired with a defective sinker. This is a single-needle problem, but it repeats continuously down the roll until that specific needle is identified and replaced.

### 3. Broken needle hook

When a needle's hook breaks off, the remaining tip becomes a sharp edge that snags and tears previously formed loops instead of catching new yarn — this typically appears as a clear puncture hole or a long rip along a stitch column, and is serious enough to require stopping the machine immediately rather than continuing to run.

### 4. Lycra/elastane faults invisible to the naked eye

Broken elastane filaments, misplating (elastane not correctly positioned relative to the base yarn), and tension variation in stretch fabric create structural loop-geometry deviations that standard visual inspection and even some automated scanners can miss — a real, growing problem as more knit programs use elastane-blended fabric for fit and stretch.

### 5. Yarn breakage from overly tight stitch settings

Short stitch lengths are sometimes chosen deliberately to increase fabric density, but if the setting is too tight, there isn't enough yarn available for the loop to release smoothly from the needle hook during knitting — the yarn breaks, and the previously formed loop opens into a hole. This is especially common on single jersey machines run at tight settings to improve fabric appearance.

### 6. Housekeeping-related stains and contamination

Oil spots, grease stains, dust marks, and fly contamination on knit fabric usually trace back to machine housekeeping — oil leaks, uncleaned material containers, inconsistent floor cleanliness — rather than a yarn or design problem, meaning these defects are a maintenance and workplace-discipline issue as much as a technical one.

### 7. Bowing and skewing from the take-up mechanism

Distortion where a pattern or line curves or runs at an angle across the fabric can originate at the knitting stage itself, from a faulty fabric take-up mechanism on the machine, rather than only from later dyeing or finishing tension (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-problems">fabric problems piece</a> for the finishing-stage version of this same defect).

### 8. Streak or stop marks

A straight horizontal line across the fabric caused by a difference in yarn tension when the knitting machine is stopped and then restarted — meaning production interruptions themselves (a maintenance pause, a shift change) can leave a visible, permanent mark in the fabric if not managed carefully.

### 9. Spirality

A specific knit-fabric distortion where the fabric's wales (vertical stitch columns) twist diagonally rather than running straight, common in certain jersey constructions and yarn types, and something that shows up specifically after washing even when the fabric looked straight on the roll.

### 10. Defects caught too late to matter

Industry estimates suggest up to 15% of circular-knit fabric ends up downgraded or scrapped specifically because defects are discovered after significant yardage has already been produced, rather than in real time — meaning the actual cost of a knitting defect is often less about the flaw itself and more about how long it ran before someone caught it.
