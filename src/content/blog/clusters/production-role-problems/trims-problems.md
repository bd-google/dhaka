### Why trims deserve their own inspection, separate from fabric and sewing

Zippers, buttons, snaps, elastic, drawcords, labels, and interlining are individually cheap components — which is exactly why they get less scrutiny than fabric or construction, and exactly why a functional trim failure is one of the most common reasons a well-made garment still fails.

### 1. Zipper malfunction

Jamming, broken teeth, or a weak pull tab turns a functional garment into an unusable one — and unlike a cosmetic defect, a broken zipper is something the customer discovers through actual use, making it a particularly damaging failure to trust and satisfaction.

### 2. Button attachment and matching problems

Loose attachment, wrong color, wrong size, or missing buttons are deceptively simple defects that still require dedicated inspection — a button that matches the approved sample in a supplier catalog can still be the wrong shade against the actual bulk fabric batch.

### 3. Weak snap fastener attachment

Snaps that separate from the fabric under normal use stress are a functional and sometimes safety-relevant defect, particularly on childrenswear and outerwear where snap failure affects fit and closure security, not just appearance.

### 4. Elastic losing recovery

Elastic that doesn't return to its original length after stretching — either from a manufacturing defect in the elastic itself or from using an elastic spec that wasn't matched to the garment's actual stretch requirements — shows up as a fit problem that develops over the garment's wear life, not always at initial inspection.

### 5. Drawcord defects and safety issues

Incorrectly inserted drawcords, missing safety features, or drawcords that don't meet destination-market safety requirements are a functional problem and, for children's garments specifically, a real compliance issue — see our <a href="/blog/kidswear-sourcing">kidswear sourcing piece</a> for the specific drawstring regulations this connects to.

### 6. Metal trim oxidation and staining

Brass zippers, buttons, and rivets can leave rust or oxidation stains directly on the fabric around them — a defect that isn't visible immediately after production but develops over time or after the garment is exposed to moisture, meaning it can pass initial inspection and still surface as a customer complaint later.

### 7. Interlining bubbling or delamination

Fusible interlining that wasn't properly bonded during application separates from the outer fabric after washing or wear, creating a visible bubbling or rippling effect — a construction-quality problem specific to interlined components like collars, cuffs, and waistbands.

### 8. Trim not matching the approved specification

A trim that's the wrong color, size, or type relative to what was approved in the tech pack — sometimes substituted by a factory trying to manage cost or a supplier's stock availability without flagging the change — produces a finished garment that technically doesn't match what the buyer signed off on, even if the substitution seems minor.

### 9. Sharp edges or corrosion risk on metal components

Metal trims with sharp edges, burrs, or corrosion risk are a safety-relevant defect requiring specific attention, particularly for garments where the trim sits close to skin or where the end product is intended for children.

### 10. Label errors

Wrong care instructions, incorrect fiber content, misbranded tags, or labels sewn in the wrong position aren't just cosmetic — mislabeling can create real compliance exposure in some destination markets (see our <a href="/blog/care-label-compliance">care label compliance piece</a>), on top of simple customer confusion.
