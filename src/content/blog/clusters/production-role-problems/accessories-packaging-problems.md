### Why packaging inspection is stricter than most other stages

A packaging error has essentially no acceptable tolerance in most buyer inspection standards — one wrong sticker or one incorrect carton count is treated as a defect the same way a torn seam would be, because packaging is the very last checkpoint before a shipment leaves for the customer, with no further stage to catch the mistake.

### 1. Wrong or off-shade hangtag

A hangtag using the wrong design, wrong information, or a shade that doesn't match the approved reference is a small physical item that still counts as a full inspection defect — buyers specifically check hangtag accuracy as part of final inspection, not as an afterthought.

### 2. Sticker and label placement errors

Most finished garments carry two or three separate stickers (size, care, brand, price), each with a specified position defined in the packing specification — using the wrong sticker, or placing the right one in the wrong spot, is counted as a defect regardless of how minor it looks.

### 3. Incorrect folding method

Buyers specify exact folding requirements (tri-fold, quad-fold, or a particular retail-ready presentation), and deviating from that spec — even if the garment itself is undamaged — is treated as a packaging defect, because retail-ready presentation is often part of what the buyer is actually paying for.

### 4. Wrong quantity or assortment per carton

Each carton has a specified quantity and size/color assortment defined by the buyer — miscounting or packing the wrong mix affects the buyer's own inventory and distribution planning downstream, making this a defect with consequences well beyond the factory floor.

### 5. Crease marks from improper folding or packing

A garment pressed and inspected perfectly can still arrive with visible creases if it was folded or packed incorrectly, or compressed too tightly in transit — packaging method directly affects the customer's actual first impression of an otherwise correctly made product.

### 6. Missing or incorrect case labels

Every carton needs a case label with PO number, style, size, quantity, and weight information — a missing case label means the carton effectively can't be identified once it reaches a warehouse or distribution center, creating real downstream logistics problems for the buyer.

### 7. Mixed purchase orders in one carton

Packing goods from two different POs into a single carton or lot is treated as a significant packaging mistake — it disrupts the buyer's receiving and inventory process and can cause an entire shipment to be flagged during receiving.

### 8. Wrong packaging material specification

Using the wrong polybag thickness or the wrong-ply cardboard for cartons isn't just a specification miss — it's a package-integrity issue that can lead to damage during shipping, particularly for cartons expected to withstand a specific stacking or handling standard.

### 9. Packaging that doesn't meet the buyer's sustainability or regulatory requirements

This is an increasingly current issue: EU buyers are moving toward requiring EN 13432-certified packaging, and multiple markets have specific plastic packaging regulations affecting standard polybags — a factory using generic packaging without confirming a specific buyer's current sustainability or regulatory requirements risks a compliance rejection that has nothing to do with the garment itself.

### 10. Shipping mark and carton marking errors

Missing required warning labels or icons, incorrect or illegible handwritten carton numbers, and marks that don't match the buyer's specific labeling requirements can each cause processing delays at the receiving warehouse — shipping marks need to be agreed with the buyer well before mass production, not assumed.
