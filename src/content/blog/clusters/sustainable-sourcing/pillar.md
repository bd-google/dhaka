### The genuinely real part: Bangladesh's green-factory infrastructure

This is one of the few "sustainability" claims in global sourcing that's fully third-party verified rather than a factory's own marketing: Bangladesh now has more LEED-certified garment factories than any other country in the world, including 9 of the world's top 10 highest-rated LEED garment facilities. This isn't a recent or superficial shift — the country went from a handful of certified factories a decade ago to hundreds now, backed by real infrastructure investment: rooftop solar arrays, zero liquid discharge water treatment, and factories like Fakir Eco Knitwears near Dhaka using daylight-optimized skylight design and AI-assisted cutting that recycles the large majority of fabric scrap back into new yarn.

Some of this is financed through Bangladesh Bank's Green Transformation Fund and the IFC's Partnership for Cleaner Textile program — meaning it's backed by real capital investment, not just a marketing claim layered on top of an unchanged factory.

### What LEED actually certifies — and what it doesn't

LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a building certification. It scores a facility's environmental footprint: energy efficiency, water use and treatment, waste management, and materials. **It says nothing about labor conditions.** A factory can be LEED Platinum-rated and still need a completely separate social compliance audit (see our <a href="/blog/compliance-certification">BSCI/WRAP/Sedex breakdown</a>) to speak to how workers are treated. Buyers who see "LEED certified" and assume that covers ethical labor practices too are checking the wrong box.

### GOTS, OEKO-TEX, and GRS — three certifications, three different questions

These get conflated constantly, so it's worth being precise:

**GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)** certifies the organic origin of the fiber and the ecological/social conditions of production across the entire chain — from certified organic cotton sourcing through spinning, dyeing, cutting, and sewing. This is the certificate that legally allows a garment to be labeled and marketed as "organic" in the EU, UK, and US.

**OEKO-TEX Standard 100** is a different question entirely — it tests the finished garment and its components for harmful substances (banned dyes, formaldehyde, heavy metals, and similar). It doesn't confirm organic origin at all; it confirms the finished product is safe against a defined chemical-safety standard. A garment can be OEKO-TEX certified without being organic, and organic without being separately OEKO-TEX tested — brands serious about both marketing claims typically require both certificates, not one as a substitute for the other.

**GRS (Global Recycled Standard)** certifies recycled material content and chain of custody — relevant if you're marketing recycled polyester or recycled cotton content specifically, which is a distinct claim from "organic."

### Why buyers are willing to pay more for this — and by how much

This isn't purely goodwill pricing. Industry sourcing conversations put the realistic premium for genuinely certified organic product at around 5% over conventional, reflecting the real cost of certified organic fiber and the chain-of-custody documentation (transaction certificates issued at each transfer stage) that legally protects the label claim downstream. That's a modest, defensible premium if the certification is real — and a red flag if a factory claims "organic" pricing without being able to produce that documentation trail.

### How to actually check a factory's sustainability claims, not just take the pitch deck

- Ask for the actual GOTS transaction certificate chain, not just a logo on a slide — organic claims are traceable by design, so a legitimate factory can produce this
- Confirm whether a "green" claim refers to the building (LEED) or the product (GOTS/GRS/OEKO-TEX) — they're unrelated certifications answering different questions
- If ethical labor claims matter to your buyer alongside environmental ones, check social compliance status separately — don't assume one implies the other
