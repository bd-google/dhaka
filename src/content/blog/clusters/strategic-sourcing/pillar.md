### Know the landscape before you pitch into it

Bangladesh has a large, genuinely capable domestic raw material base for certain categories — knit fabric, basic trims, standard accessories — built over three decades of backward-linkage investment. It has real, structural gaps in others — raw cotton, woven fabric, and technical/synthetic fiber specifically (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap breakdown</a>). A foreign supplier who understands which category they're walking into can pitch intelligently; one who doesn't ends up either underselling a genuine advantage or overselling into a market that's already well served locally.

### Two different conversations, depending on the category

If you supply something Bangladesh already produces well domestically — standard knit fabric, generic buttons, basic packaging — your value proposition has to be about something other than being marginally cheaper, because domestic suppliers have a real logistics and pricing edge on their home turf. Quality consistency, a specific certification, or a capability gap in the local offering are more realistic angles than price competition.

If you supply something in a genuine structural gap — technical yarn, specialty dyes, certified organic/recycled inputs, woven fabric at a spec local mills don't run — the conversation is much more straightforward, because the demand already exists without a local alternative to compete against.
