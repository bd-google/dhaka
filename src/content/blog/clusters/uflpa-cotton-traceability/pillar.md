### The part of this law almost every first-time buyer gets wrong

The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA) is often misunderstood as a China-specific import restriction — meaning many buyers sourcing from Bangladesh assume it doesn't apply to them. It does. UFLPA creates a legal presumption that any goods containing material mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in China's Xinjiang region are made with forced labor and therefore banned from US entry — and critically, **this is about where the raw material came from, not where the garment was sewn.** A Bangladesh cut-and-sew factory using cotton or yarn that traces back to Xinjiang produces a finished garment that falls squarely under UFLPA, even though the factory itself has no connection to China at all.

### The scale of enforcement is real, not theoretical

Since the law took effect in mid-2022, US Customs and Border Protection has detained more than 2,200 shipments of apparel, textiles, and footwear — valued at nearly $92 million — specifically over insufficient cotton origin verification. Detentions rose roughly 25% in 2024 compared to the prior year, averaging over 400 a month. This is described by trade compliance specialists as operational law enforced daily, not a distant legal risk.

### What "rebuttable presumption" actually means for you

The default legal assumption is that your shipment is forced-labor product. The burden of proof is entirely on the importer — you have to demonstrate your goods aren't connected to Xinjiang or a listed entity, not the other way around. If CBP detains a shipment, you generally have 30 days (extendable to a maximum of 90) to submit documentary evidence, or face exclusion, seizure, or a requirement to export or destroy the goods.

### The documentation CBP actually expects

- **A full supply-chain map** — every facility involved from raw cotton through finished garment, named specifically: the cotton-growing region or farm, the ginning mill, the spinning mill, the knitter or weaver, the dye house, and the cut-and-sew factory
- **Commercial documents** for each transaction along that chain — invoices, bills of lading, and packing lists connecting each stage to the next
- **Supporting evidence** — third-party audits, relevant certifications (GOTS, Organic Content Standard, Better Cotton Initiative), and where available, forensic testing (isotopic or DNA-based cotton origin verification, technologies CBP itself uses and encourages importers to adopt)

### Why this specifically matters for Bangladesh sourcing

Bangladesh imports the large majority of its raw cotton and a meaningful share of its yarn (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap piece</a>), and some of that supply chain has touched Chinese cotton and yarn markets historically. That doesn't mean your specific supply chain is affected — but it does mean "my factory is in Bangladesh, not China" is not, on its own, a compliance answer. You need to know where your cotton actually originated, not just where your garment was sewn.

### What to actually ask your factory for, in plain terms

- A named-origin declaration for every cotton-containing product, signed on factory letterhead
- A clear answer to who their yarn spinner is, who that spinner's cotton source is, and where that cotton was actually grown
- A commitment that if a shipment is detained, the factory will provide supporting documentation within a specific, short window (7 days is a reasonable ask, matching how compliance-focused buyers in this space already operate)

A factory that can't or won't provide this isn't ready to supply the US market today — and factories that already sell to larger, established brands typically have this data on hand already, because those buyers have been requiring it since 2022.
