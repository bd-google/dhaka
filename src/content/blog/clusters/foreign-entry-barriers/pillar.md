### The gap between interest and actual presence

Bangladesh draws real interest from foreign suppliers — the market is large, the garment industry's raw material and machinery needs are substantial, and the opportunity is genuine (see our <a href="/blog/fabric-yarn-gap">fabric and yarn gap piece</a> for one clear example). And yet foreign direct investment into Bangladesh has stayed under 1% of GDP in recent data — a genuinely low figure for a market this size, and specifically flagged by international trade assessments as a sign something structural is holding entry back, not just a lack of interest.

### The bureaucratic maze, specifically

Establishing a registered presence involves multiple, separate approval layers that don't always move in parallel: company registration through the Registrar of Joint Stock Companies and Firms (RJSC), a BIDA registration process requiring detailed project plans and financial documentation, and — for regulated sectors specifically — additional clearances from relevant ministries. Trade barrier assessments have specifically noted overlapping administrative procedures and a lack of transparency in how these processes actually move, which is a different problem from the rules simply being strict — it's that the rules aren't always predictable in application.

### The problem hiding inside "profit repatriation is legal"

Bangladeshi law technically permits repatriation of profits and external payments. In practice, foreign exchange rules and their inconsistent application have been specifically identified by research (citing World Bank analysis) as a primary obstacle to foreign investment — meaning the gap isn't in what the law says, it's in how reliably and quickly it works in practice. A foreign supplier planning around the assumption that repatriation is simple because it's technically legal is planning around an incomplete picture.

### The instability nobody warns you about: staff turnover inside the approving agencies

This is a specific, underappreciated barrier: frequent transfers of top- and mid-level officials across Bangladeshi ministries and directorates disrupt continuity on pending approvals — a project moving through a multi-month approval process can find itself restarting parts of that process when the official who understood the file moves to a different post. This isn't corruption or bad faith; it's an administrative pattern that specifically punishes anyone expecting a Western-style continuous case-officer relationship.

### The harder-to-quantify barriers

- **Land acquisition** — a specifically flagged difficulty for foreign investors setting up physical operations, tied to unclear property rights and a slower transfer process than most foreign entrants expect
- **Corruption** — explicitly named in a recent US Trade Representative assessment as deeply embedded enough in the commercial environment to affect ordinary business transactions, not just large infrastructure deals
- **Logistics and infrastructure** — port congestion and inconsistent power supply (see our <a href="/blog/factory-operational-risks">factory operational risks piece</a>) add real cost and unpredictability on top of the regulatory barriers
- **Policy inconsistency** — frequently changing tax rules specifically undermine the long-term planning a foreign supplier needs to justify establishing a real presence rather than working through intermediaries indefinitely
